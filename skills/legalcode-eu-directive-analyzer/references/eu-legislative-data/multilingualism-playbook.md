# Multilingualism Playbook — Equal Authenticity, Reconciliation, Operational Rules

This playbook is the authoritative guide to handling multiple language versions
of EU acts across the seven-skill EU Legislative Compliance suite. The rules
below are anchored in primary law (Regulation 1/1958), the equal-authenticity
doctrine of the Court of Justice (*CILFIT*, *Codan*, *EMU Tabac*, *Endendijk*),
and the EEA additions for Icelandic and Norwegian. Every conformity verdict,
every gold-plating finding, and every obligation register the suite emits must
honour the doctrine — assuming the English text is canonical is an Anti-Pattern
flagged in every skill in the suite.

---

## 1. Regulation 1/1958 and the 24 official languages

### 1.1 The original instrument

Council Regulation No 1 determining the languages to be used by the European
Economic Community (Reg. 1/1958, OJ 17, 6.10.1958, p. 385) is the foundational
language regulation for the Union. CELEX `31958R0001`. ELI:
`https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/1958/1(1)/oj/eng`.

The operative rules, in their current consolidated form:

- **Art. 1** — the official languages and the working languages of the institutions
  of the Union shall be the languages listed (currently 24).
- **Art. 4** — regulations and other documents of general application shall be
  drafted in the official languages.
- **Art. 5** — the *Official Journal of the European Union* shall be published in
  the official languages.
- **Art. 7** — the languages to be used in the proceedings of the Court of Justice
  shall be laid down in its rules of procedure.

Regulation 1/1958 is itself amended on every accession; the current 24-language
list is the cumulative product of every accession treaty since 1973.

## 2. The EEA addition — Icelandic and Norwegian

The EEA Agreement extends the equal-authenticity principle to Icelandic and
Norwegian for purposes of the EEA legal order, but with carefully limited scope.

- **Art. 129(1) EEA** — The EEA Agreement is drawn up in a single original in
  Czech, Danish, Dutch, English, Estonian, Finnish, French, German, Greek,
  Hungarian, Icelandic, Italian, Latvian, Lithuanian, Maltese, Norwegian, Polish,
  Portuguese, Romanian, Slovak, Slovenian, Spanish, Swedish, Bulgarian, and
  Croatian, with the texts in each of these languages being equally authentic.
- **Joint Committee Decisions** — JCDs incorporating EU acts into the EEA Annexes
  are published in all EEA Agreement languages, including Icelandic and Norwegian.
  The Icelandic and Norwegian texts are authentic for purposes of EEA law.

**Important limitation**: Icelandic and Norwegian are **not authentic** in
EU-internal litigation. A reference for a preliminary ruling under Art. 267 TFEU
from a Member State court interpreting an act incorporated into the EEA may not
treat the Icelandic or Norwegian text as authoritative on the EU side. The EFTA
Court, conversely, treats Icelandic and Norwegian as fully authentic for EEA
EFTA-pillar disputes under Art. 31 SCA.

The EEA-incorporation-tracker skill consumes this distinction: when a JCD
incorporates an EU act and the Icelandic translation reveals a divergence from
the English text, that divergence is binding on Icelandic operators and
Icelandic courts but does not back-propagate into EU-internal interpretation.

## 3. The equal-authenticity doctrine — operative case law

### 3.1 *CILFIT* — Case C-283/81, ECLI:EU:C:1982:335

*Srl CILFIT and Lanificio di Gavardo SpA v Ministry of Health* established two
foundational propositions cited in every language-version dispute at the Court:

1. **Equal authenticity**: "It must be borne in mind, even where the different
   language versions are entirely in accord with one another, that Community law
   uses terminology which is peculiar to it. Furthermore, it must be emphasised
   that legal concepts do not necessarily have the same meaning in Community law
   and in the law of the various Member States" (CILFIT, para. 19).

2. ***Acte clair* limit on Art. 234 EC (now Art. 267 TFEU) referral**: a national
   court of last instance may refrain from referring only where it has compared
   "the various language versions" and is satisfied that the matter is equally
   obvious to the courts of the other Member States and to the Court of Justice
   itself (CILFIT, paras. 16–21).

Operationally, *CILFIT* is the source of the rule that **multilingual comparison
is a precondition of confident legal interpretation**, not an optional cross-check.

### 3.2 *Codan* — Case C-236/97, ECLI:EU:C:1998:208

*Skatteministeriet v Aktieselskabet Forsikringsselskabet Codan* held that, where
language versions of a Community provision diverge, "the provision in question
must be interpreted by reference to the purpose and general scheme of the rules
of which it forms part" (Codan, para. 28). Reconciliation is therefore not a
choice between the texts; it is a teleological synthesis.

The operative passage:

> "The need for a uniform interpretation of those versions makes it necessary,
> in the case of divergence between them, for the provision in question to be
> interpreted by reference to the purpose and general scheme of the rules of
> which it forms part" (Codan, para. 28).

### 3.3 *EMU Tabac* — Case C-296/95, ECLI:EU:C:1998:152

*EMU Tabac Sàrl v Imperial Tobacco Ltd* ruled that "the wording used in one
language version of a Community provision cannot serve as the sole basis for the
interpretation of that provision, or be made to override the other language
versions in that regard. Such an approach would be incompatible with the
requirement of uniform application of Community law" (EMU Tabac, para. 36).

This is the proposition that defeats the temptation to treat the English version
of a directive as canonical merely because English is the *de facto* working
language of the Council and Commission. **No one language version dominates.**

### 3.4 *Endendijk* — Case C-187/07, ECLI:EU:C:2008:197

*Criminal proceedings against Dirk Endendijk* applied the *Codan* methodology to
agricultural law: the Court held that where the German, Dutch, and English
versions of a directive on the protection of calves diverged on the meaning of
"tethered", the provision had to be interpreted by reference to the purpose of
the directive (animal welfare) and its general scheme (Endendijk, paras. 22–24).

The case is the modern operative example: when the conformity-assessment skill
flags a definitional divergence between authentic versions, *Endendijk* is the
authority for treating the divergence not as a defect to be ignored but as a
signal that purposive interpretation must do work the literal reading cannot.

## 4. Reconciliation methodology — when language versions diverge

The four-step methodology below is mandatory whenever the suite identifies a
material divergence between authentic language versions. Skipping any step and
proceeding on the basis of a single language is an Anti-Pattern.

### Step 1: Compare all 24 versions

Retrieve every `/oj/<lang>` Expression of the relevant article. The Cellar SPARQL
query in `source-playbook.md` § 1.1(b) returns the full set in a single call.
Identify the definitional or scope term in dispute. Catalogue the meanings across
versions.

### Step 2: Group the versions

Cluster the 24 versions by semantic alignment. Typically the divergence reveals
two or three clusters: e.g. for NIS2 Art. 3 "essential entities" versus
"important entities", the English `essential` aligns with French `essentiel`,
German `wesentlich`, Spanish `esencial`, Italian `essenziale`, but the
Slovenian and Croatian versions translate differently. Group by linguistic and
legal-tradition cluster.

### Step 3: Apply the *Codan*/*Endendijk* purposive test

Reconcile the clusters by reference to the **purpose** and the **general scheme**
of the directive. The recitals and the explanatory memorandum of the Commission
proposal are admissible interpretative materials at this step (settled CJEU
practice; see e.g. *Schul* C-461/03). The reconciled meaning is the meaning that
best gives effect to the act's stated objective across all language clusters.

### Step 4: Document the divergence and the reconciliation

The conformity-assessment skill must record, in its Glass Box audit trail:
- the article and term in dispute,
- the divergent renderings in all 24 versions (with ELI `/oj/<lang>` URIs),
- the cluster grouping,
- the recital references invoked under *Codan*,
- the reconciled interpretation,
- the resulting verdict implication.

Skipping the documentation step destroys the audit trail that allows a national
court (or the Commission, in an Art. 258 procedure) to follow the suite's
reasoning. Every reconciliation must be reproducible from the trail alone.

### 4.1 Worked example — NIS2 Art. 3 "essential entities" vs. "important entities"

Article 3 of the NIS2 Directive (Directive (EU) 2022/2555, CELEX 32022L2555,
ELI `https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/dir/2022/2555/oj/eng`) classifies entities
within scope as either "essential" (Art. 3(1) entities listed in Annex I) or
"important" (Art. 3(2) entities listed in Annex II). The distinction matters
because supervision regimes differ (ex-ante for essential, ex-post for important
under Art. 32 NIS2).

- English: `essential entities` / `important entities`.
- French: `entités essentielles` / `entités importantes`.
- German: `wesentliche Einrichtungen` / `wichtige Einrichtungen`.
- Spanish: `entidades esenciales` / `entidades importantes`.
- Italian: `soggetti essenziali` / `soggetti importanti`.
- Polish: `podmioty kluczowe` / `podmioty ważne` — note `kluczowe` literally
  "key", a slightly stronger rendering than `essential`.
- Croatian: `ključni subjekti` / `važni subjekti` — same divergence as Polish.

The clusters: Romance + Germanic + English use a near-identical
"essential/important" pair; Slavic-language Expressions use "key/important".
Applying *Codan*: the recitals and the general scheme make clear the distinction
is regulatory-supervisory (ex-ante vs. ex-post oversight intensity), not
substantively about the entity's qualitative importance. The "key/important"
rendering produces the same operational classification as "essential/important".
No verdict-affecting divergence; document and proceed.

This is the kind of cross-version check the conformity-assessment skill must run
on every term that affects scope, deontic strength, or sanction.

## 5. Practical rules

### 5.1 When to use `eng` only

For **internal analysis**, the suite operates in the user's working language. The
analyzer skill extracts obligations from the user's working language Expression.
Where the user has not specified, default to `eng`.

For **definitive analysis** — conformity verdicts, gold-plating findings,
infringement timelines that may be cited in regulatory submissions — comparison
across all 24 authentic versions is mandatory whenever the verdict turns on a
definitional or scope term.

### 5.2 Terminology drift over time

Translations are not frozen at adoption. The Publications Office issues
*corrigenda* (corrigendum acts in the OJ L series, e.g. "Corrigendum to Directive
(EU) 2022/2555") that retroactively correct translation errors. Always fetch the
latest consolidated Expression at the relevant point in time, not the
adoption-date Expression. The Cellar `eli:point-in-time` mechanism described in
`identifier-playbook.md` § 6.1 is the controlled mechanism.

### 5.3 IATE cross-checking

When a divergence is suspected, IATE (see `source-playbook.md` § 7) is the
authoritative terminology cross-check. An IATE entry that lists all 24 language
renderings with reliability code 4 (highest, "verified by Publications Office
terminologists") is dispositive evidence that the renderings are intended
equivalents. A reliability code below 3 on a critical term is a red flag.

### 5.4 EEA-relevance flag

Every act marked "Text with EEA relevance" in its OJ publication line is
candidate for EEA incorporation. The flag does not by itself add Icelandic and
Norwegian as authentic versions for EU-internal litigation; it does mean the
JCD process under Art. 102 EEA will produce Icelandic and Norwegian Expressions
for use in IS/NO/LI implementation. The EEA-incorporation-tracker skill is the
appropriate path when this matters.

## 6. Operational guidance for the analyzer skill

The analyzer skill (`legalcode-eu-directive-analyzer`) is the foundation of the
suite. Its language handling rule:

1. **Extract obligations from the user's working language.** Default `eng`. The
   working-language Expression is the basis for the obligation register.

2. **Set `language-divergence-flag = unverified` on every obligation by default.**
   The flag records that no cross-language reconciliation has been performed.

3. **For obligations that turn on a defined term, scope clause, or deontic
   modal**, run the four-step reconciliation methodology and update the flag to
   `verified-aligned` (no material divergence) or `verified-divergent` (material
   divergence found and documented).

4. **For obligations addressed to undertakings established in a Member State
   whose authentic language is not the working language**, set
   `addressee-authentic-language = <iso-code>` on the obligation. The conformity
   assessment skill consumes this flag to know which Expression to compare against
   the NIM.

5. **For acts marked EEA-relevant**, set `eea-relevance = true` and link to the
   EEA-incorporation-tracker skill output (or note the JCD has not yet been
   adopted, in which case Icelandic and Norwegian Expressions do not yet exist).

The downstream skills inherit these flags. The conformity-assessment skill, for
example, treats a `verified-divergent` flag as a precondition to issuing a
verdict — it cannot rule that a Member State has correctly transposed an obligation
whose meaning is itself contested across language versions without first
documenting the reconciliation.

This is what equal authenticity requires in operational practice.
