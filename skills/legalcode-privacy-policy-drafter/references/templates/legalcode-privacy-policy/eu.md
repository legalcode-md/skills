### 2.2 Defined terms

We use defined terms in the GDPR sense. In particular:

- **Personal data** — any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person (GDPR Art. 4(1)).
- **Special category data** — personal data revealing racial or ethnic origin, political opinions, religious or philosophical beliefs, trade union membership, genetic data, biometric data used for unique identification, health data, or data concerning sex life or sexual orientation (GDPR Art. 9(1)).
- **Processing** — any operation performed on personal data, automated or not (Art. 4(2)).
- **Controller** — the person or body that decides the purposes and means of processing (Art. 4(7)).
- **Processor** — a person or body that processes personal data on behalf of the controller (Art. 4(8)).
- **Data subject** — the identified or identifiable natural person whose personal data is being processed.
- **Subprocessor** — a processor engaged by a processor to carry out specific processing activities on behalf of a controller.

- Providing customer support that is necessary to deliver the contracted service (purpose §6.2).

- Responding to lawful orders from competent authorities (purpose §6.9).
- Notifying supervisory authorities and, where required, data subjects of personal data breaches under Arts. 33–34 (purpose §6.4).

### 7.3 Legitimate interests — Art. 6(1)(f)

We rely on our legitimate interests, or those of a third party, where those interests are not overridden by your interests or fundamental rights and freedoms. Before relying on this basis, we carry out a balancing test (a "legitimate interests assessment", or LIA) that considers the necessity of the processing, its impact on you, your reasonable expectations, and any safeguards we apply. We can provide you with a summary of any LIA on request.

The legitimate interests we rely on include:

- **Securing the service against abuse and attack** (purpose §6.4) — our and our users' interest in a service that is not compromised by fraud, malware, or denial-of-service.
- **Maintaining service performance and reliability** (purpose §6.5) — our interest in operating a stable service, our users' interest in availability.
- **Improving the service** through privacy-friendly product analytics that do not rely on cookies or cross-site tracking (purpose §6.6).
- **Sending service-related communications** (purpose §6.7) — our interest in keeping users informed about a product they actively use.
- **Direct marketing to existing customers** for similar products and services where permitted by national law (purpose §6.8). You can object at any time (see Section 14).
- **Defending and exercising legal claims** (purpose §6.9) — our interest in being able to assert and defend rights.
- **Evaluating and completing corporate transactions** (purpose §6.10) — our interest, and that of counterparties, in being able to negotiate and complete transactions, with personal data limited to what is necessary and protected by confidentiality.

You have the right to object to any processing based on Art. 6(1)(f); see Section 14.

### 7.4 Consent — Art. 6(1)(a)

### 7.5 Vital interests — Art. 6(1)(d)

We may rely on vital interests in rare emergencies — for example, processing limited data to alert emergency services where there is a risk to life. We do not rely on this basis for routine processing.

### 7.6 Public task — Art. 6(1)(e)

We do not generally rely on public task. Where we cooperate with public authorities, we rely on legal obligation (Art. 6(1)(c)) or, where appropriate, legitimate interests (Art. 6(1)(f)).

## 9. International data transfers

Where possible, we host and process EU/EEA personal data inside the EU/EEA. Some of our processors and subprocessors are nevertheless established in third countries. This Section explains the safeguards we apply.

### 9.1 Adequacy decisions — Art. 45

For transfers to countries that the European Commission has decided offer an adequate level of protection, we rely on the relevant adequacy decision. Adequacy decisions are currently in force for, among others, the United Kingdom, the EEA states (Iceland, Norway, Liechtenstein), Switzerland, Japan (commercial operators), the Republic of Korea, Israel, Argentina, New Zealand, Andorra, the Faroe Islands, Guernsey, Jersey, the Isle of Man, and Uruguay. Adequacy is also recognised for transfers to certified organisations in the United States under the EU-US Data Privacy Framework, where the recipient maintains a valid certification.

### 9.2 Standard Contractual Clauses — Art. 46(2)(c)

Where no adequacy decision applies, we rely on the European Commission's Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) approved in Implementing Decision (EU) 2021/914. We choose the appropriate Module for the transfer:

- **Module 1 — controller to controller (C2C)** — between two independent controllers.
- **Module 2 — controller to processor (C2P)** — where we transfer to a non-EEA processor.
- **Module 3 — processor to processor (P2P)** — where a non-EEA subprocessor is engaged.
- **Module 4 — processor to controller (P2C)** — where we, as processor, transfer back to a non-EEA controller.

Where {{Service Name}} is also offered to UK data subjects, we attach the UK International Data Transfer Addendum (IDTA) to the SCCs, or use the UK IDTA standalone, so that the same transfer mechanism covers both regimes.

### 9.3 Supplementary measures (Schrems II)

Following *Schrems II* (CJEU C-311/18), we carry out a Transfer Impact Assessment (TIA) for transfers under Art. 46. The TIA looks at the laws and practices of the destination country, the nature of the data, the duration of processing, and the technical, contractual, and organisational supplementary measures we apply. Typical measures include encryption in transit and at rest with keys held in the EU/EEA, pseudonymisation, strict access controls, transparency reporting, and contractual commitments to challenge unlawful access requests.

### 9.4 Binding Corporate Rules — Art. 47

*OPTIONAL — only if {{Company Short Name}} or any of its subprocessors rely on BCRs.*

For transfers within a corporate group, we may rely on Binding Corporate Rules approved by a competent supervisory authority in accordance with Art. 47.

### 9.5 Derogations — Art. 49

We do not rely on Art. 49 derogations as a routine transfer mechanism. We may rely on a derogation in specific situations, such as a transfer that is necessary for the performance of a contract you have requested, or where you have given explicit consent after being informed of the risks.

## 10. Retention

We retain personal data only for as long as we need it for the purposes in Section 6 and for any period the law requires.

### 10.1 Retention principles

Our retention is governed by the following principles:

- **Storage limitation** — we keep personal data in identifiable form no longer than necessary (Art. 5(1)(e)).
- **Documented schedule** — every category of data has a documented retention period in our internal retention schedule.
- **Deletion or anonymisation at the end** — at the end of the retention period we delete the data or irreversibly anonymise it.

### 10.2 Indicative periods

The actual retention period for any given record depends on the data category and the legal basis. Indicative periods:

- **Account data** — for the lifetime of the account, plus a short tail (typically 30–90 days) to allow account recovery, then deletion.
- **Billing and accounting records** — *OPTIONAL — only if {{Service Name}} is paid.* For the period required by tax law (commonly 7–10 years in the EU; in Germany, see Section 18).
- **Support correspondence** — typically up to 24 months after closure of the ticket, unless a longer period is necessary for an open issue.
- **Security logs** — typically 90–180 days, longer where an investigation is open or where required by law.
- **Backups** — for the rotation period of the backup system (commonly up to 35 days), after which deletions in the live system propagate to backups by overwriting.
- **Marketing data** — *OPTIONAL — only if marketing is conducted.* Until you opt out or, in the absence of activity, after a defined period of inactivity.
- **Records of consent and opt-outs** — for as long as we rely on the consent, plus a reasonable period afterwards to evidence compliance.

We can provide more specific periods on request.

## 11. Security measures

We implement technical and organisational measures (TOMs) appropriate to the risk in accordance with GDPR Art. 32. Our TOMs cover at least the following areas:

### 11.1 Confidentiality

- Role-based access control with the principle of least privilege.
- Multi-factor authentication for staff access to production systems.
- Encryption in transit (TLS 1.2+ for all external traffic) and at rest (AES-256 or equivalent for stored data).
- Network segmentation between production, staging, and corporate environments.

### 11.2 Integrity

- Tamper-evident audit logging for privileged operations.
- Code review for all changes to production code paths.
- Cryptographic signing of build artefacts.
- Schema and migration controls so changes to data are deliberate and reviewed.

### 11.3 Availability

- Geographically redundant hosting within the EU/EEA where the data classification requires it.
- Documented backup and restore procedures, regularly tested.
- Capacity, latency, and error-rate monitoring with alerting.

### 11.4 Resilience

- Periodic disaster-recovery and chaos exercises.
- Independent third-party security testing (penetration testing) at least annually.
- A vulnerability disclosure policy and a process for handling external security reports.

### 11.5 Personnel & vendors

- Confidentiality undertakings from all staff and contractors.
- Security awareness and data protection training, refreshed at least annually.
- Vendor risk assessment before onboarding any processor that will handle personal data.

### 11.6 Breach notification

We do not subject you to decisions based solely on automated processing, including profiling, that produce legal effects concerning you or similarly significantly affect you, unless one of the Art. 22(2) exceptions applies.

### 15.2 Where automated decision-making does occur

*OPTIONAL — keep this subsection only if you carry out solely automated decisions that have legal or similarly significant effects, such as automated credit decisions, automated abuse blocking that suspends an account, or automated content moderation that demonetises or removes content.*

Where automated decisions are made (for example, automated abuse detection that may temporarily suspend an account), we do so on one of the following bases:

- **Necessary for the performance of, or entry into, a contract** with you (Art. 22(2)(a)), where the decision is essential to operate the service safely.
- **Authorised by EU or member-state law** (Art. 22(2)(b)) that lays down suitable safeguards.
- **Your explicit consent** (Art. 22(2)(c)).

### 15.3 Safeguards

In every case where we make a solely automated decision with legal or similarly significant effects, we ensure the safeguards required by Art. 22(3):

- The right to obtain **human intervention** on the part of a competent reviewer.
- The right to **express your point of view**.
- The right to **contest the decision**.

You can exercise these rights by writing to {{Company Email Privacy}} and explaining the decision you want reviewed. We will provide a human review without undue delay.

### 15.4 Profiling

Where we use profiling that does not produce legal or similarly significant effects (for example, low-impact personalisation), we still apply the principles of necessity, transparency, and the right to object under Art. 21.

## 18. Germany-specific addenda (BDSG / DSGVO) — *regional appendix*

> This section is a regional appendix. It supplements (and does not replace) Sections 1–17 for users and customers in Germany. The 17-section structure above is the primary policy.

The German Federal Data Protection Act (Bundesdatenschutzgesetz, "BDSG") and the German Telecommunications-Telemedia Data Protection Act (Telekommunikation-Telemedien-Datenschutzgesetz, "TTDSG") apply alongside the GDPR and provide additional rules of national law that affect how we process personal data of individuals in Germany.

### 18.1 Special category processing on public-interest grounds — §22 BDSG

Where we process special category data on a basis other than consent (for example, to defend against attacks that involve biometric or health data, or to comply with employment-law obligations of an employer-customer), we rely on the appropriate condition in §22 BDSG together with Art. 9(2) GDPR. We apply the safeguards required by §22(2) BDSG, including access restrictions, encryption, pseudonymisation, audit logging, designation of responsible persons, and processing-purpose limitation.

### 18.2 Employment-context data — §26 BDSG

*OPTIONAL — only if {{Service Name}} processes employment-context data of {{Company Short Name}}'s own employees, or if {{Company Short Name}} processes employment data on behalf of an employer-customer in the role of processor.*

§26 BDSG governs the processing of employees' personal data for purposes of the employment relationship. Where this policy is read by employees of {{Company Short Name}}, the employment-context aspects of processing (recruitment, payroll, working-time, performance, exit) are described in our internal employee privacy notice and in the works-council agreements that apply.

Where {{Company Short Name}} processes employment data on behalf of an employer-customer, the employer is the controller and §26 BDSG applies between the employer and its employees; our role is that of processor under a Data Processing Agreement.

For broader employment-data clauses (recruitment, performance management, surveillance limits, works-council interplay), refer to the data-protection clauses of `legalcode-employment-agreement-de`.

### 18.3 Cookies and similar technologies — §25 TTDSG

In Germany, the storage of, or access to, information on a user's terminal equipment is governed by §25 TTDSG, which transposes Art. 5(3) of the ePrivacy Directive. The TTDSG requires:

- **Consent before storage or access**, except where the storage or access is strictly necessary to provide a telemedia service that the user has expressly requested (§25(2)(2) TTDSG).
- **Transparent information** about the purpose of the storage or access and any access by third parties, given before consent is requested.
- **Equal weight for "reject"** in the consent banner — German supervisory authorities (in particular the DSK) have repeatedly held that "Accept all" and "Reject all" must be equally prominent and equally easy to use.

Section 12 of this policy describes how we comply with these requirements; the companion `legalcode-cookie-policy` provides the full cookie inventory.

### 18.4 Works-council interplay

*OPTIONAL — only if {{Company Short Name}} has its own works council, or processes employment data on behalf of customers that have one.*

In Germany, the introduction or modification of technical systems that can monitor employees' performance or behaviour is, as a rule, subject to co-determination under §87(1)(6) of the Works Constitution Act (Betriebsverfassungsgesetz, "BetrVG"). This affects features such as detailed audit logging, productivity analytics, or screen-recording. We support our customers in concluding works-council agreements (Betriebsvereinbarungen) where required and provide processing-detail documentation suitable for that process.

### 18.5 Inventor compensation / employee inventors note

*OPTIONAL — only if {{Service Name}} touches on R&D output or if {{Company Short Name}} is itself an employer of inventors in Germany.*

Processing of employee-inventor information (for example, names of inventors on patent filings) is subject to both the GDPR and the German Employee Inventions Act (Gesetz über Arbeitnehmererfindungen, "ArbnErfG"). We process inventor-name and inventor-share data on the legal basis of legal obligation (Art. 6(1)(c) GDPR) and contract performance (Art. 6(1)(b)) and retain it for the periods required by patent and inventor-compensation rules.

### 18.6 Supervisory authority — Germany

The competent supervisory authority depends on where the controller has its establishment in Germany.

- **Federal-scope matters** (federal authorities, telecommunications and postal services for federal companies) — the Bundesbeauftragte für den Datenschutz und die Informationsfreiheit (BfDI), https://www.bfdi.bund.de.
- **Most companies (private sector)** — the Land-level data protection authority (Landesbeauftragte für Datenschutz, "LDA") of the Bundesland in which the controller has its principal establishment.

For {{Company Short Name}}, the relevant Land-level supervisory authority is **the Landesbeauftragte für Datenschutz of {{Company State / Bundesland}}**.

A complete list of the Land authorities is maintained by the BfDI: https://www.bfdi.bund.de/DE/Service/Anschriften/Laender/Laender-node.html. The German data protection authorities also coordinate informally as the Datenschutzkonferenz (DSK), which publishes guidance at https://www.datenschutzkonferenz.de.

### 18.7 Data Protection Officer triggers — §38 BDSG

*OPTIONAL — informational only.*

In Germany, in addition to the GDPR triggers in Art. 37 GDPR, §38 BDSG requires the appointment of a DPO where, among other things, at least 20 persons are constantly engaged in automated processing of personal data. Where this trigger applies to {{Company Short Name}}, our DPO contact is given in Section 3.1.

### 18.8 German bookkeeping retention

*OPTIONAL — only if {{Service Name}} is paid and {{Company Short Name}} is subject to German bookkeeping rules.*

German tax and commercial law (in particular §147 AO and §257 HGB) typically require retention of accounting records and commercial letters for between 6 and 10 years. Where retention under Section 10 of this policy would otherwise be shorter, we apply the longer statutory period to the records concerned and restrict processing to the purpose of complying with that obligation.
