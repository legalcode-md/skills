### 2.1 Post-Brexit context

The UK GDPR is the United Kingdom's domestic version of the EU General Data Protection Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2016/679), incorporated into UK law on 1 January 2021 by the European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018 and amended by the Data Protection, Privacy and Electronic Communications (Amendments etc) (EU Exit) Regulations 2019. It sits alongside the Data Protection Act 2018, which provides the UK-specific derogations, exemptions, and supplementary rules that the GDPR leaves to national law.

The UK GDPR is substantively similar to the EU GDPR but is enforced by the ICO under a separate UK regime. References in this policy are to the UK GDPR unless we are specifically describing a transfer to or from the European Economic Area, in which case the EU GDPR may also be relevant.

### 2.2 Definitions used in this policy

We use the same vocabulary as the UK GDPR and the DPA 2018:

- **Personal data** — any information relating to an identified or identifiable living individual (UK GDPR Art. 4(1)). This includes obvious identifiers (name, email, account ID) and less obvious ones that can identify someone in combination (IP address, device identifier, cookie ID, account-internal user reference).
- **Special category data** — personal data revealing racial or ethnic origin, political opinions, religious or philosophical beliefs, trade union membership, genetic data, biometric data processed for unique identification, health data, or data concerning sex life or sexual orientation (UK GDPR Art. 9(1)).
- **Criminal offence data** — personal data relating to criminal convictions and offences, or related security measures (UK GDPR Art. 10; DPA 2018 s.10 and Sch. 1).
- **Processing** — any operation performed on personal data, automated or not (Art. 4(2)) — collection, recording, storage, use, disclosure, erasure, and so on.
- **Controller** — the party that determines the purposes and means of processing (Art. 4(7)).
- **Processor** — a party that processes personal data on behalf of a controller (Art. 4(8)).
- **Subprocessor** — a vendor a processor engages to help carry out the controller's instructions (Art. 28(2)–(4)).
- **Data subject** ("you") — the identified or identifiable individual to whom the personal data relates.
- **Information society service** — a service normally provided for remuneration, at a distance, by electronic means, and at the individual request of a recipient (the term used in UK GDPR Art. 8 and the Children's Code).

Where this policy uses everyday English ("we share", "we keep", "we let you opt out"), assume the equivalent technical UK GDPR meaning.

## 7. Lawful bases for processing

For each purpose in Section 6, we identify the lawful basis we rely on under UK GDPR Art. 6(1). Where we process special category data (Art. 9) or criminal offence data (Art. 10 and DPA 2018 s.10), we identify a further condition.

### 7.1 The six lawful bases under UK GDPR Art. 6(1)

The UK GDPR allows processing only where at least one of the following applies:

- **(a) Consent** — you have given clear, freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous consent for the processing (Art. 6(1)(a) and Art. 7). You can withdraw consent at any time, and withdrawal is as easy as giving it.
- **(b) Contract** — the processing is necessary for the performance of a contract to which you are a party, or in order to take steps at your request before entering into a contract (Art. 6(1)(b)).
- **(c) Legal obligation** — the processing is necessary for compliance with a legal obligation to which we are subject under UK or retained EU law (Art. 6(1)(c)).
- **(d) Vital interests** — the processing is necessary to protect someone's life or other vital interests (Art. 6(1)(d)). This is a narrow basis we expect to use only in emergencies.
- **(e) Public task** — the processing is necessary for the performance of a task carried out in the public interest or in the exercise of official authority (Art. 6(1)(e)). Public task is generally available to public bodies; we do not normally rely on it.
- **(f) Legitimate interests** — the processing is necessary for the legitimate interests pursued by us or by a third party, except where those interests are overridden by your interests, rights, and freedoms (Art. 6(1)(f)). Public authorities cannot rely on this basis for tasks they perform in their public capacity.

Where we rely on legitimate interests, we carry out a three-part **Legitimate Interests Assessment (LIA)** following ICO guidance:

1. **Purpose test** — is there a legitimate interest behind the processing?
2. **Necessity test** — is the processing necessary to achieve that interest, or could we achieve it less intrusively?
3. **Balancing test** — does the legitimate interest override the individual's interests, rights, and freedoms, taking into account their reasonable expectations?

You can object to processing based on legitimate interests at any time (see Section 14). If you object, we will stop unless we can demonstrate compelling legitimate grounds that override your interests, or unless we need the processing for the establishment, exercise, or defence of legal claims.

## 9. International data transfers

The Service is operated from {{Company Country}} and uses cloud infrastructure that may store and process personal data in countries outside the United Kingdom. Where personal data is transferred outside the UK, we ensure that the transfer is covered by an appropriate Chapter V UK GDPR safeguard.

### 9.1 Adequacy regulations

A transfer to a country covered by a UK adequacy regulation does not require a further safeguard. As of the last updated date of this policy, the United Kingdom recognises the following destinations under adequacy regulations or equivalent retained EU adequacy decisions:

- The European Economic Area (EEA) — the 27 EU Member States plus Iceland, Liechtenstein, and Norway.
- Andorra, Argentina, the Faroe Islands, Guernsey, the Isle of Man, Israel, Jersey, New Zealand, Switzerland, Uruguay.
- Japan (private sector only, scope as defined by the relevant adequacy regulation).
- The Republic of Korea (South Korea).
- Canada (commercial organisations subject to PIPEDA — partial adequacy).
- The United States — for entities certified under the **UK Extension to the EU–US Data Privacy Framework** (the "UK–US Data Bridge"), within the scope of the Data Bridge.

The list of UK adequacy regulations is updated by the UK Government from time to time; we monitor changes and adjust our transfers accordingly. The current list is published on `gov.uk` and `ico.org.uk`.

### 9.2 Transfers to non-adequate countries — appropriate safeguards

Where a transfer is to a country not covered by an adequacy regulation, we rely on one of the appropriate safeguards in UK GDPR Art. 46:

- **UK International Data Transfer Agreement (IDTA)** — the UK-native standalone transfer contract issued under DPA 2018 s.119A. We use the IDTA where SCCs are not the appropriate instrument and the transfer is direct under UK GDPR.
- **UK Addendum to the EU SCCs** — where our counterparty has signed the EU 2021/914 Standard Contractual Clauses, we attach the **International Data Transfer Addendum to the EU Commission Standard Contractual Clauses** so the SCCs operate as a valid UK transfer mechanism.
- **Binding corporate rules (BCRs)** — *OPTIONAL — only if you have approved UK BCRs.* Approved by the ICO under UK GDPR Art. 47.
- **Approved codes of conduct or certification schemes** — under UK GDPR Arts. 40 and 42.

### 9.3 Transfer Risk Assessment (TRA)

Following the *Schrems II* judgment of the Court of Justice of the EU (which is retained case law in the UK to the extent it relates to retained EU law), and consistent with the ICO's Transfer Risk Assessment guidance, we carry out a Transfer Risk Assessment for transfers to non-adequate countries. The TRA examines:

- The nature of the personal data and the purpose of the transfer.
- The legal regime in the destination country, including law-enforcement and intelligence access powers, and the rights and remedies available to data subjects.
- The technical, contractual, and organisational measures we and the importer apply to bring the level of protection up to the UK GDPR standard (for example encryption in transit and at rest, pseudonymisation, split-key custody, and challenge procedures for government access requests).

Where the TRA shows that the importer cannot honour the transfer mechanism in practice, we either suspend the transfer or apply additional safeguards until it can.

### 9.4 Derogations — UK GDPR Art. 49

In rare cases where neither an adequacy regulation nor an Art. 46 safeguard is available, we may rely on an Art. 49 derogation — for example, the transfer is necessary for the performance of a contract with you, for the establishment, exercise, or defence of legal claims, or with your explicit informed consent. Derogations are used sparingly and only where the law permits.

## 13. Children's data

The Service is not directed at children under 13. We do not knowingly collect personal data from a child under 13.

### 13.1 Age of consent — UK GDPR Art. 8

UK GDPR Art. 8(1), as modified by DPA 2018 s.9, sets the minimum age at which a child can consent to information society services in the United Kingdom at **13** (the UK chose the lower threshold permitted by the GDPR; the EU default is 16). Where consent is the lawful basis for offering an information society service to a child under 13, we obtain consent from a person with parental responsibility for that child, and we make reasonable efforts to verify that consent.

### 13.2 Age Appropriate Design Code (Children's Code)

Where the Service is an information society service likely to be accessed by children, we design and operate it in line with the ICO's **Age Appropriate Design Code** (the "Children's Code"), a statutory code issued under DPA 2018 s.123. The fifteen standards in the Code include:

1. Best interests of the child as a primary consideration.
2. Carrying out a Data Protection Impact Assessment to assess and mitigate risks to children.
3. Age-appropriate application of standards (with risk-based approach to age estimation or robust age-assurance).
4. Transparency in language and presentation children can understand.
5. No detrimental use of personal data (no using children's data in a way that has been shown to be detrimental to their wellbeing).
6. Adherence to our published policies and community standards.
7. High-privacy default settings.
8. Data minimisation tailored to children's services.
9. No data sharing by default unless we can demonstrate a compelling reason in the child's best interests.
10. Geolocation off by default; visible indicator when location tracking is active.
11. Parental controls clearly signposted, with clear cues to the child when parental monitoring is active.
12. No "nudge" techniques pushing children towards lower-privacy choices or longer engagement.
13. Connected toys and devices subject to the same standards.
14. Online tools available to help children exercise their data protection rights.
15. Data Protection Impact Assessments informed by these standards and reviewed when the Service changes.

If you believe a child has provided us with personal data and you have parental responsibility for that child, contact {{Company Email Privacy}} and we will investigate and, where appropriate, delete the data.

## 14. Your rights & how to exercise them

Under the UK GDPR (Arts. 12–22) and Part 2 of the Data Protection Act 2018, you have the rights described below. The exact conditions for each right, the timelines we have to respond, and any limited exemptions are set by statute.

### 14.1 The rights

- **Right to be informed (Arts. 13–14)** — to receive clear information about how we process your personal data. This policy is the principal way we meet that right.
- **Right of access — Subject Access Request (SAR) (Art. 15)** — to obtain confirmation that we process your personal data and a copy of it, plus the supplementary information described in Art. 15.
- **Right to rectification (Art. 16)** — to have inaccurate personal data corrected and incomplete personal data completed.
- **Right to erasure ("right to be forgotten") (Art. 17)** — to have your personal data deleted in certain circumstances, including when it is no longer necessary, when you withdraw consent, or when you successfully object to processing.
- **Right to restriction of processing (Art. 18)** — to ask us to limit the processing of your personal data while a question about it is resolved.
- **Right to data portability (Art. 20)** — where we process your personal data on the basis of consent or contract and by automated means, to receive that data in a structured, commonly used, machine-readable format and to have it transmitted to another controller where technically feasible.
- **Right to object (Art. 21)** — to object at any time to processing based on legitimate interests or public task, including profiling based on those grounds; and to object at any time to processing for direct marketing, including any related profiling. Once you object to direct marketing, we will stop.
- **Rights in relation to automated decision-making and profiling (Art. 22)** — see Section 15.
- **Right to withdraw consent (Art. 7(3))** — where we rely on your consent, to withdraw it at any time. Withdrawal does not affect the lawfulness of processing already carried out on the basis of your consent.
- **Right to lodge a complaint (Art. 77)** — to complain to the Information Commissioner's Office (ICO), the UK supervisory authority, at `ico.org.uk` (see Section 17).

### 14.2 How to exercise your rights

You can exercise any right by writing to {{Company Email Privacy}} or, where we provide one, using the in-product self-service tool (for example, an in-app data export or deletion option).

We will:

1. Acknowledge your request.
2. Verify your identity using information we already hold, so that we do not disclose data to the wrong person.
3. Respond within **one calendar month** of receiving the request (Art. 12(3)). Where the request is complex or you have made a number of requests, we may extend this period by a further two months and will tell you about the extension within the original month, with reasons.
4. Tell you, where we cannot fulfil all or part of your request, the reason and any further steps you can take, including the right to complain to the ICO.

You can ask someone to make a request on your behalf. We may ask for evidence of their authority before we act.

### 14.3 Fees

We will not charge you a fee for exercising your rights, except as permitted by Art. 12(5) where the request is **manifestly unfounded or excessive** (in particular, where it is repetitive). In those cases, we may either charge a reasonable fee taking into account the administrative costs or refuse to act. We will tell you which we have chosen and explain why.

Where we apply an exemption, we will tell you that we have done so to the extent the exemption itself does not prevent us from doing so.

## 15. Automated decision-making & profiling

We do not make decisions based **solely** on automated processing — including profiling — that produce legal effects concerning you or similarly significantly affect you, except in the limited cases permitted by UK GDPR Art. 22(2): where the decision is necessary for entering into or performing a contract, where it is authorised by UK or retained EU law, or where you have given your explicit consent.

We do use automated processing for:

- Spam, abuse, and fraud detection.
- Security signals (for example, detecting suspicious sign-in attempts, denial-of-service patterns, or compromised credentials).
- Service-improvement analytics described in Section 6.
- *OPTIONAL — only if you do this.* Personalising content or recommendations within the Service.

These activities involve **profiling** within the meaning of UK GDPR Art. 4(4), but they do not normally produce legal or similarly significant effects on you, and they are not solely automated decisions within Art. 22.

Where any automated process could, in your situation, qualify as automated decision-making with significant effects, we will:

- Tell you about it at the point of collection (Arts. 13(2)(f) and 14(2)(g)).
- Provide meaningful information about the logic involved, in language you can understand.
- Tell you the significance and the envisaged consequences for you.
- Give you the right to obtain human intervention, to express your point of view, and to contest the decision.

If you have questions about a specific automated process, write to {{Company Email Privacy}}. We follow the ICO's guidance on automated decision-making and profiling when designing these systems.
