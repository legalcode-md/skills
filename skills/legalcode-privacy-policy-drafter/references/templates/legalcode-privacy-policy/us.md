## 2. Scope & definitions

This policy covers personal information collected through **{{Service Name}}** at **{{Service URL}}** and through related communications, support channels, and account pages.

**What this policy does not cover**

- Third-party websites, integrations, or services that you reach from {{Service Name}}. Those are governed by their own notices.
- Information we process strictly as a service provider or processor on behalf of a business customer under a written contract that meets CCPA §1798.140(ag). For that processing, the business customer is the controller; our role and obligations are set out in our data processing agreement with that customer.
- Aggregated or de-identified information that cannot reasonably be linked to you, which the CCPA expressly excludes from the definition of "personal information" (§1798.140(v)(3)).
- Protected health information ("PHI") that we receive under a HIPAA Business Associate Agreement. PHI is handled under the BAA, not this policy.

**Key terms used in this policy**

- **Personal information** — information that identifies, relates to, describes, is reasonably capable of being associated with, or could reasonably be linked, directly or indirectly, with a particular consumer or household (CCPA §1798.140(v)). The European concept of "personal data" maps to this term; this policy uses "personal information" except in international transfer language.
- **Sensitive personal information** — the categories listed in CCPA §1798.140(ae): government identifiers; account log-in credentials with the password or security answer; precise geolocation; race or ethnic origin; religious or philosophical beliefs; union membership; the contents of mail, email, or text messages where we are not the intended recipient; genetic data; biometric information used for unique identification; and information concerning health, sex life, or sexual orientation.
- **Consumer** — a natural person who is a resident of a state that grants them privacy rights under that state's law.
- **Sale** — the disclosure of personal information for monetary or other valuable consideration to a third party (CCPA §1798.140(ad)).
- **Sharing** — the disclosure of personal information to a third party for cross-context behavioral advertising, whether or not for monetary consideration (CCPA §1798.140(ah)).
- **Service provider / contractor / processor** — a person who processes personal information on our behalf under a written contract that restricts the recipient's use of the information.
- **Third party** — a recipient that is not a service provider, contractor, or processor.

## 4. Personal information we collect

This section describes the personal information we collect when you use {{Service Name}}. (Statutory note: California uses the term "personal information"; the EU/UK use "personal data" — Section 2 explains the mapping.)

We collect personal information in three ways: directly from you, automatically when you interact with the service, and from a small set of third parties listed in Section 5.

### 4.1 Information you give us

- **Account data** — name, email address, password (stored as a salted hash), and any optional profile fields you choose to add (for example, an avatar, a job title, a time zone, or a display name).
- **Billing data** — for paid plans, billing name, billing address, the country and tax-status of the bill-payer, and the last four digits of the payment method. Full payment card numbers go directly to our payment processor; we do not store them.
- **Communications data** — the content of emails, support tickets, and feedback you send us, plus any attachments and the metadata of those messages.
- **User content** — files, prompts, queries, documents, and other inputs that you submit to the service. Please avoid putting sensitive personal information in user content unless the feature you are using is designed for it.

### 4.2 Information we collect automatically

- **Device and connection information** — IP address, browser type and version, operating system, device identifiers, time zone, language preference, and referring URLs.
- **Usage information** — pages viewed, features used, links clicked, search terms entered into our own site search, session timing, and error reports.
- **Cookies and similar technologies** — see Section 12 for the full list and your choices.
- **Approximate geolocation** — derived from IP address, used to localize content and detect fraud. We do not collect precise geolocation (within a 1,850-foot radius) unless you explicitly turn on a feature that requires it; if we did, that would be sensitive personal information under CCPA §1798.140(ae)(1)(G).
- **Security telemetry** — log entries needed to detect, prevent, and respond to malicious activity (for example, repeated failed log-ins, anomalous request rates, indicators of credential stuffing).

### 4.3 Information from third parties

- **Identity providers** — if you log in with a single sign-on provider (for example Google or Microsoft), we receive your name, email address, and a unique identifier for the SSO session.
- **Payment processors** — confirmation of payment, transaction status, the country of the payment method, and risk signals.
- **Anti-fraud and security vendors** — risk scores and indicators for traffic that triggers our fraud rules.
- **Public sources** — for example, public business registries, when needed to verify a B2B customer's identity.

### 4.4 Sensitive personal information

We collect sensitive personal information only when we need it for one of the purposes that CCPA §1798.121(a) treats as exempt from the right to limit (for example, account security and fraud prevention), and only the categories listed below:

- **Account credentials** — your email address combined with the salted hash of your password and any multi-factor authentication secrets.

We do not collect biometric information, precise geolocation, government identifiers, racial or ethnic origin, religious or philosophical beliefs, union membership, message contents, genetic data, or information concerning health, sex life, or sexual orientation through {{Service Name}}, unless an optional feature you have explicitly turned on requires it. If we ever introduce such a feature, we will update this policy and obtain consent where required.

We do not use sensitive personal information to infer characteristics about you. (CCPA §1798.121(d).)

## 10. Retention

This section explains how long we keep personal information. (CCPA §1798.100(a)(3) requires that we tell you the length of time we intend to retain each category, or the criteria used to determine that period.)

We keep personal information for as long as we need it for the purposes set out in Section 6, and then we delete or de-identify it. The default retention windows are:

- **Account data** — for the life of the account, plus 30 days after the account is closed (to allow account recovery), then deleted or de-identified. Backup retention may extend this by up to 35 days.
- **Billing records** — 7 years after the transaction, to satisfy United States federal and state tax and accounting requirements.
- **Communications and support tickets** — 3 years from the last interaction.
- **Security and audit logs** — 12 months from the date of the log entry, except logs that record a security incident, which we keep for 7 years.
- **User content** — for as long as the account is active; deleted within 30 days of account closure (subject to backup retention).
- **Marketing list entries** — until you unsubscribe, plus a suppression-list record kept indefinitely so that we can honor your unsubscribe.
- **Cookies** — see Section 12 for cookie-by-cookie expiry.

If a longer period is required by a legal hold, an open dispute, or a regulatory obligation, we keep the relevant records for as long as that obligation lasts, then delete them.

## 11. Security measures

This section describes how we protect personal information. (CCPA §1798.150 ties security obligations to a private right of action for certain breaches.)

We use administrative, technical, and physical safeguards designed to protect personal information against unauthorized access, alteration, disclosure, or destruction. These include:

- **Encryption** — TLS 1.2 or higher in transit and AES-256 at rest for data stored in our primary databases and object stores.
- **Access control** — least-privilege role-based access; multi-factor authentication for production access; just-in-time elevation for sensitive operations.
- **Network security** — segmented networks, web application firewalls, and DDoS protection at the edge.
- **Application security** — code review, static analysis, dependency scanning, and a documented vulnerability disclosure process.
- **Operational security** — centralized logging and monitoring; an incident response plan; regular tabletop exercises.
- **People security** — background checks where allowed by law; mandatory privacy and security training for all staff; written confidentiality obligations.
- **Vendor security** — risk-based diligence on subprocessors; written data protection terms; periodic re-assessment.

No security program is perfect. If we ever experience a breach involving the unencrypted and unredacted personal information described in CCPA §1798.150(a)(1), we will notify you without unreasonable delay and consistent with the requirements of state breach-notification laws (for California, Cal. Civ. Code §1798.82).

## 12. Cookies & similar technologies

This section explains the cookies and similar technologies we use, and your choices — including the Global Privacy Control and Do Not Track signals.

### 12.1 What we use and why

### 12.2 Your choices

You can:

- Block or delete cookies through your browser settings. The service's account features will not work if you block strictly necessary cookies.
- Install the analytics opt-out provided by our analytics vendor, where one is available.

### 12.3 Global Privacy Control (GPC)

We honor the Global Privacy Control browser signal as a valid request to opt out of sale and sharing under CCPA §1798.135(b) and Cal. Code Regs. tit. 11, § 7025. When we receive a GPC signal from a browser or device:

- We treat the signal as a verified request to opt out of sale and sharing for that browser/device, and where possible for the consumer if logged in.
- We confirm receipt and the action we have taken, where the regulations require.
- We do not require any additional log-in or step to honor the signal.

### 12.4 Do Not Track (DNT)

Most browsers send a "Do Not Track" header. The state of the law has not converged on a single meaning for DNT, so we do not change our practices based on the DNT header alone. Where the law treats GPC as the operative signal, we honor GPC as described in 12.3.

## 14. Your rights & how to exercise them

This section explains the privacy rights you have and how to use them.

If you are a California resident, the CCPA gives you the rights listed in 14.1. If you live in another state with a comprehensive privacy law (Colorado, Connecticut, Oregon, Texas, Utah, Virginia), the rights you have are the operative ones in 14.1 with the variations noted in 14.4–14.9.

### 14.1 Rights for California residents (CCPA/CPRA)

- **Right to know** — request the categories of personal information we have collected, the categories of sources, the business or commercial purposes for collection, the categories of third parties with whom we have disclosed it, and the specific pieces of personal information we hold about you. (CCPA §§ 1798.110, 1798.115.) The right to know covers the 12-month period before your request, except for personal information collected on or after 2022-01-01, which you can request without that 12-month limit (§1798.130(a)(2)(B)).
- **Right to delete** — request deletion of personal information we have collected from you, subject to the exceptions in §1798.105(d). (§1798.105.)
- **Right to correct** — request that we correct inaccurate personal information. We will use commercially reasonable efforts to correct it. (§1798.106.)
- **Right to opt out of sale or sharing** — direct us not to sell or share personal information about you. (§1798.120.) You can submit this request at **{{Do Not Sell URL}}**, by emailing {{Company Email Privacy}}, or by sending a Global Privacy Control signal from your browser. We do not currently sell or share personal information; if our practice changes, this opt-out will be operative before the change takes effect.
- **Right to limit use and disclosure of sensitive personal information** — direct us to use sensitive personal information only for the purposes that §1798.121 treats as exempt from the right to limit. (§1798.121.) We currently do not use sensitive personal information beyond those exempt purposes, so this right does not change our practice; you can still submit a request and we will confirm.
- **Right to data portability** — receive the personal information you request under the right to know in a structured, commonly used, and machine-readable format that you can transmit to another business. (§1798.130(a)(2).)
- **Right to non-discrimination** — exercise any of these rights without us denying service, charging different prices, providing a different level of service, or suggesting that we will. (§1798.125.) We may, however, offer a financial incentive (a different price or service level) if it is reasonably related to the value of the data and you opt in.

### 14.2 How to make a request

You can submit a request in any of the following ways:

- **Online form** — {{DSAR Submission URL}}.
- **Email** — {{Company Email Privacy}}, with "Privacy Request" in the subject line and the right you are exercising in the body.
- **Account controls** — many requests (download, delete, correct) can be completed in the account settings page in {{Service Name}} without filing a separate request.
- **Global Privacy Control** — for opt-out of sale and sharing, send a GPC signal from your browser; we honor it for the browser and, where you are logged in, for your account.

We need to verify your identity before we act on a request to know, delete, or correct, to make sure we are returning your information to you. Verification involves matching information you give us in the request to the information we hold; in some cases we may ask for a signed declaration under penalty of perjury (§1798.130(a)(7)). For a request to opt out of sale or sharing or to limit sensitive personal information, we do not require identity verification — but we may take reasonable steps to confirm that the request is authentic.

We respond to the appeal within the period required by the relevant state law (typically 60 days). If we deny the appeal, we tell you how to contact the state attorney general.

### 14.6 Colorado residents (CPA)

The Colorado Privacy Act (C.R.S. §§ 6-1-1301 to 6-1-1313, effective 2023-07-01) gives Colorado residents the rights to access, correct, delete, port, and opt out of sale, targeted advertising, and certain profiling. Colorado requires us to honor a universal opt-out mechanism (currently, GPC) as a valid opt-out signal for sale and targeted advertising (4 C.C.R. 904-3, Rule 5.06). Colorado adds a specific right to opt out of profiling in furtherance of decisions that produce legal or similarly significant effects; we describe our profiling practice in Section 15 (we do not currently engage in such profiling). Colorado denials can be appealed within the period set by the Attorney General; we respond to appeals within 45 days, with a 60-day extension where reasonably necessary.

### 14.7 Virginia residents (VCDPA)

The Virginia Consumer Data Protection Act (Va. Code §§ 59.1-575 to 59.1-585, effective 2023-01-01) gives Virginia residents the rights to access, correct, delete, port, and opt out of sale of personal data, targeted advertising, and certain profiling. Virginia does not currently require recognition of a universal opt-out signal for those opt-outs; you can submit an opt-out request through the channels in 14.2. Virginia residents have a right to appeal a denial within a reasonable period; we respond within 60 days. Virginia limits the right to delete to data the controller has obtained from the consumer (rather than data inferred or collected from a third party).

### 14.8 Connecticut residents (CTDPA)

The Connecticut Data Privacy Act (Public Act No. 22-15, Conn. Gen. Stat. §§ 42-515 to 42-525, effective 2023-07-01) gives Connecticut residents the rights to access, correct, delete, port, and opt out of sale, targeted advertising, and profiling in furtherance of decisions producing legal or similarly significant effects. Since 2025-01-01, Connecticut requires controllers to honor a universal opt-out mechanism — we treat GPC as that mechanism for opt-out of sale and targeted advertising. Appeals are available within the period set by the Attorney General; we respond within 60 days.

### 14.9 Utah residents (UCPA)

The Utah Consumer Privacy Act (Utah Code §§ 13-61-101 to 13-61-404, effective 2023-12-31) is narrower in scope than the CCPA: B2B contacts and employee data are excluded, and there is no right to correct. Utah residents have rights to access, delete, port, and opt out of sale and targeted advertising. Utah does not require recognition of a universal opt-out signal and does not provide a statutory appeals mechanism.

### 14.10 Texas residents (TDPSA)

The Texas Data Privacy and Security Act (Tex. Bus. & Com. Code §§ 541.001 to 541.205, effective 2024-07-01) tracks the VCDPA closely. Texas residents have rights to access, correct, delete, port, and opt out of sale, targeted advertising, and certain profiling. Since 2025-01-01, Texas requires recognition of a universal opt-out signal for opt-out of sale and targeted advertising — we treat GPC as that signal. Texas requires us to disclose, where applicable, that we sell sensitive personal data or biometric data; we do neither.

### 14.11 Oregon residents (OCPA)

The Oregon Consumer Privacy Act (Or. Rev. Stat. §§ 646A.570 to 646A.589, effective 2024-07-01) gives Oregon residents rights to access, correct, delete, port, and opt out of sale, targeted advertising, and certain profiling. Oregon adds a distinctive right to obtain a list of the specific third parties to whom the controller has disclosed personal data (or, at the controller's option, the specific third parties to whom the controller has disclosed any personal data). We provide that list in response to a verified request submitted under 14.2.

### 14.12 No discrimination, no fees

We do not charge a fee for responding to verifiable consumer requests, unless the request is manifestly unfounded or excessive (in particular because of its repetitive character), in which case we may charge a reasonable fee or refuse to act, and we will tell you why. (CCPA §1798.145(g)(3); see also state equivalents.)

## 15. Automated decision-making & profiling

This section explains whether we make decisions about you using automated processing.

We do not make decisions that produce legal or similarly significant effects about you using solely automated processing — *unless an explicit clause in this policy says otherwise.*

We do use limited automated processing to:

- **Detect fraud and abuse** — for example, scoring sign-ups and log-in attempts to filter automated attacks. The output is a risk signal that informs human decisions; sustained denial of service requires human review.
- **Filter spam** — for example, in support inboxes.
- **Recommend content within the product** — for example, suggesting documentation. Suggestions do not affect your access to the service.

These uses are not "profiling in furtherance of decisions producing legal or similarly significant effects" under CCPA §1798.140(z), the California regulations on automated decisionmaking technology (Cal. Code Regs. tit. 11, §§ 7200–7222), Colorado's Rule 9 (4 C.C.R. 904-3, Rule 9), or the equivalent provisions in Connecticut, Oregon, Texas, and Virginia.

If we ever introduce automated decision-making with legal or similarly significant effects, we will:

- Update this policy to describe the logic, the categories of personal information used, and the consequences.
- Where the law provides, give consumers the right to opt out of the profiling and the right to request human review.
- Conduct a data protection assessment / risk assessment as required by the relevant state law (for example, CCPA Risk Assessment regulations at Cal. Code Regs. tit. 11, §§ 7150–7158; Colorado data protection assessments under C.R.S. § 6-1-1309).
