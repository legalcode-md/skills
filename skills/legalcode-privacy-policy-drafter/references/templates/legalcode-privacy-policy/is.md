### 2.1 Iceland's data-protection framework

Iceland implements the GDPR through **Act No. 90/2018 on Data Protection and the Processing of Personal Data** ("Persónuverndarlög"). The Act incorporates the GDPR into Icelandic law on the basis of Iceland's membership in the European Economic Area (EEA) under the EEA Agreement and the Joint Committee Decision adopting the GDPR for the EEA. As a result, the substantive rules that apply to processing of personal data in Iceland are the GDPR rules, supplemented by national provisions in Act 90/2018 (for example, on age of digital consent, scientific research, and the powers of the supervisory authority).

The supervisory authority responsible for enforcing data-protection law in Iceland is **Persónuvernd**, the Icelandic Data Protection Authority, based in Reykjavík (see Section 17).

### 2.2 Definitions used in this policy

- **Personal data (persónuupplýsingar)** — any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person, as defined in GDPR Art. 4(1) and Act 90/2018 Art. 3(2). This includes obvious identifiers (name, kennitala, email, account ID) and identifiers that can identify someone in combination (IP address, device fingerprint, cookie ID, location data).
- **Special categories of personal data (viðkvæmar persónuupplýsingar)** — the categories listed in GDPR Art. 9(1) and Act 90/2018 Art. 3(3): racial or ethnic origin, political opinions, religious or philosophical beliefs, trade-union membership, genetic data, biometric data processed to uniquely identify a person, health data, and data concerning sex life or sexual orientation. Iceland treats these categories with heightened protection.
- **Processing (vinnsla)** — anything we do with personal data: collecting, storing, organising, using, sharing, transferring, deleting.
- **Controller (ábyrgðaraðili)** — the party that determines the purposes and means of processing.
- **Processor (vinnsluaðili)** — a party that processes personal data on a controller's instructions.
- **Subprocessor (undirvinnsluaðili)** — a vendor a processor engages to help carry out the controller's instructions.
- **Data subject (skráður einstaklingur)** — the individual to whom the personal data relates. In this policy, "you" usually means the data subject.
- **Persónuvernd** — the Icelandic Data Protection Authority; the supervisory authority for Iceland.
- **Kennitala** — the Icelandic national identifier (10-digit ID number) issued by Þjóðskrá Íslands. We treat kennitala as personal data; we do not collect kennitala unless we need it for an identified statutory purpose (for example, tax or AML).

Where regional law uses different wording for these concepts, we treat the regional terms as equivalent for the purposes of this policy.

## 7. Legal bases for processing

Iceland's Act 90/2018 implements the GDPR; the Act and the GDPR share the same Art. 6 / Art. 9 framework, with Iceland-specific provisions for certain national matters. We document a specific lawful basis for every processing activity, as required by GDPR Art. 6(1) and Act 90/2018 Art. 9.

### 7.1 Lawful bases under GDPR Art. 6 / Act 90/2018 Art. 9

We rely on one or more of the following bases. Many activities engage more than one basis; where we rely on more than one, we document the primary basis.

**(a) Consent** — GDPR Art. 6(1)(a) and Art. 7; Act 90/2018 Art. 9(1)(1) and Art. 10. We rely on your freely-given, specific, informed, and unambiguous consent for activities such as optional marketing communications, optional analytics cookies, and any optional feature where consent is the appropriate basis. You can withdraw consent at any time with effect for the future (Section 14).

**(b) Performance of a contract with you** — GDPR Art. 6(1)(b); Act 90/2018 Art. 9(1)(2). We rely on this basis to provide the core Service to you (account creation and authentication, delivering features you request, paid-plan administration where applicable). Where there is no contract yet, we rely on this basis for steps taken at your request before entering into a contract.

**(c) Compliance with a legal obligation** — GDPR Art. 6(1)(c); Act 90/2018 Art. 9(1)(3). The relevant legal obligations are those imposed on us by Icelandic or EEA law that applies to us as an Icelandic-established controller. Examples include:

- tax record-keeping under the Act on Income Tax No. 90/2003 and supporting tax procedure rules;
- accounting record-keeping under the Act on Annual Accounts No. 3/2006 and the Act on Bookkeeping No. 145/1994;
- anti-money-laundering and counter-terrorist-financing checks under Act No. 140/2018 (where applicable);
- responding to lawful requests from Persónuvernd, the Icelandic Tax Authority (Skatturinn), and other Icelandic authorities;
- reporting obligations under Act 90/2018 (for example, personal data breach notification under GDPR Art. 33–34).

**(d) Vital interests** — GDPR Art. 6(1)(d); Act 90/2018 Art. 9(1)(4). We rely on this basis only in exceptional cases where processing is necessary to protect the vital interests of you or another natural person — for example, contacting emergency services in response to a credible safety report.

**(e) Public task / public interest** — GDPR Art. 6(1)(e); Act 90/2018 Art. 9(1)(5). This basis is generally relevant only to public bodies and to private bodies carrying out a task in the public interest. {{Company Short Name}} does not normally rely on this basis. *OPTIONAL — retain only if your service genuinely involves a public task assigned by Icelandic law.*

**(f) Legitimate interests** — GDPR Art. 6(1)(f); Act 90/2018 Art. 9(1)(6). We rely on legitimate interests where processing is necessary for purposes that we (or a third party) pursue and those interests are not overridden by your interests, fundamental rights, or freedoms. For each such activity we apply a three-part test:

1. **Purpose test** — is there a real and legitimate interest?
2. **Necessity test** — is the processing actually necessary to achieve that interest, with no less intrusive alternative?
3. **Balancing test** — do our interests outweigh the impact on your rights, taking into account your reasonable expectations and the safeguards we apply?

We document each balancing test (legitimate interests assessment) and review it periodically. Typical legitimate-interest activities for us include security monitoring and abuse prevention, network and information security, internal administrative purposes, and improvement of existing features using minimised data. You have the right to object to legitimate-interests processing at any time (Section 14).

This basis is **not** available to public authorities for processing in the performance of their tasks (GDPR Art. 6(1) final paragraph).

## 9. International data transfers

Iceland is a member of the **European Economic Area (EEA)**. Personal data flows freely between Iceland and other EEA states (the EU Member States plus Norway and Liechtenstein); these are not "international transfers" for the purposes of Chapter V of the GDPR.

Transfers of personal data **outside the EEA** are subject to the safeguards in GDPR Chapter V (Art. 44–49), which apply in Iceland through Act 90/2018. We rely on one or more of the following mechanisms when we transfer personal data outside the EEA:

### 9.1 Adequacy decisions — Art. 45

Where the European Commission has adopted an adequacy decision in respect of a destination country (and that decision has been incorporated into the EEA Agreement), we may transfer personal data to that country without further safeguards. Current adequacy decisions include the United Kingdom, Switzerland, Andorra, Argentina, Canada (commercial organisations), Faroe Islands, Guernsey, Israel, Isle of Man, Japan, Jersey, New Zealand, the Republic of Korea, and Uruguay. Iceland follows EEA adequacy determinations in practice; verify the current list before adoption.

For transfers to **the United States**, we rely on the **EU–U.S. Data Privacy Framework (DPF)** for transfers to organisations that are certified under the DPF, where the DPF adequacy decision applies in Iceland through the EEA. Where the recipient is not DPF-certified, we use Standard Contractual Clauses (Section 9.2).

For transfers to **the United Kingdom**, we rely on the UK adequacy decision (current as of the last update of this policy) and equivalent EEA recognition.

### 9.2 Standard Contractual Clauses — Art. 46(2)(c)

For transfers to countries that do not benefit from an adequacy decision, we rely on the **Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)** adopted by the European Commission in Implementing Decision (EU) 2021/914, in the module appropriate to the controller-to-controller, controller-to-processor, processor-to-processor, or processor-to-controller relationship.

For UK-originating transfers (where the UK GDPR rather than the EU/EEA GDPR is the applicable regime), we use the **UK International Data Transfer Agreement (IDTA)** or the **UK Addendum** to the EU SCCs.

We carry out a **transfer impact assessment** (mat á flutningi gagna) for SCC-based transfers, evaluating local laws and practices that could affect the importer's ability to comply with the SCCs, in line with EDPB Recommendations 01/2020 and Persónuvernd guidance on transfers (`personuvernd.is`).

### 9.3 Other safeguards — Art. 46

Other Art. 46 safeguards we may rely on include **Binding Corporate Rules** (Art. 47), approved codes of conduct, and approved certification mechanisms. We will indicate in the relevant subprocessor entry which safeguard applies.

### 9.4 Derogations — Art. 49

We use derogations under Art. 49 only where no other safeguard is available, sparingly, and only where the law permits. The most relevant derogations are explicit informed consent (Art. 49(1)(a)), necessity for a contract concluded in your interest (Art. 49(1)(b) / (c)), and necessity for the establishment, exercise, or defence of legal claims (Art. 49(1)(e)).

## 10. Retention

We keep personal data only as long as we need it for the purpose we collected it for, then we delete or de-identify it, in line with the storage limitation principle in GDPR Art. 5(1)(e) and Act 90/2018 Art. 8.

The actual retention period for a given record depends on:

- the purpose it was collected for (Section 6);
- whether we still have an active relationship with you;
- any legal requirement that obliges us to keep the record (for example, the seven-year retention requirement for accounting and tax records under the Act on Bookkeeping No. 145/1994 and the Act on Annual Accounts No. 3/2006);
- whether we need to retain the record to defend against legal claims within the applicable limitation period under Icelandic civil law.

As a guide:

| Data | Indicative retention |
|---|---|
| Account record | Lifetime of the account, plus a short tail for closure, dispute resolution, and applicable limitation periods. |
| Content you create or upload | Until you delete it, or until your account is closed and the closure grace period ends. |
| Billing and tax records | Seven years from the end of the relevant accounting year, in line with Icelandic bookkeeping and tax law. |

| Server and security logs | Short retention windows (typically 30–365 days) unless an incident requires longer for investigation. |
| Marketing preferences | Until you opt out, plus a suppression record so we honour your opt-out going forward. |
| Support tickets | Up to a few years after closure of the ticket, depending on severity and any related legal hold. |

When we no longer need personal data, we delete it or convert it into a form that no longer identifies you. Backups are overwritten on rolling cycles; data we cannot immediately purge from a backup is isolated and deleted at the next cycle.

## 11. Security measures

We use a layered set of technical and organisational measures appropriate to the risk involved in processing your personal data, as required by GDPR Art. 32 and Act 90/2018 Art. 27. These measures include:

- **Encryption in transit** — TLS for all network traffic to and from the Service.
- **Encryption at rest** — for data stored on disk by our infrastructure providers.
- **Access controls** — role-based access for staff, multi-factor authentication for privileged systems, and least-privilege defaults.
- **Audit logging** — production access is logged and reviewed.
- **Vendor due diligence** — we assess subprocessors' security posture before engaging them and at periodic intervals afterwards.
- **Backups and recovery** — encrypted backups with documented restore procedures.
- **Vulnerability management** — patching, dependency scanning, and a process for receiving and acting on security reports.
- **Pseudonymisation and minimisation** — where feasible, personal data is pseudonymised or minimised at the point of collection.
- **Incident response** — a documented procedure for investigating, containing, and notifying affected parties and Persónuvernd in the event of a personal data breach.

## 13. Children's data

The Service is not directed to children under the age of digital consent set by Icelandic law.

Iceland exercised the discretion in GDPR Art. 8(1) to lower the age of digital consent below the GDPR default of 16. Under **Act 90/2018 Art. 17**, the age at which a child can consent to information-society services on their own behalf is **13**. Below this age, processing of a child's personal data on the basis of consent (GDPR Art. 6(1)(a)) requires the consent or authorisation of the holder of parental responsibility.

We do not knowingly collect personal data from children below the age of 13 without verifiable consent from a holder of parental responsibility. We make reasonable efforts to verify, where the law requires it, that such consent has been given.

If you believe a child has given us personal data without the necessary consent, contact {{Company Email Privacy}} and we will investigate and, where appropriate, delete the data.

## 14. Your rights & how to exercise them

Under the GDPR (as it applies in Iceland through Act 90/2018), you have the following rights as a data subject (skráður einstaklingur). The Icelandic terminology used in legal practice is included in parentheses for reference.

- **Right of access (aðgangsréttur) — GDPR Art. 15.** You can ask us to confirm whether we process personal data about you and, if so, to provide a copy together with the information described in Art. 15.
- **Right to rectification (réttur til leiðréttingar) — GDPR Art. 16.** You can ask us to correct inaccurate personal data about you and to complete incomplete data.
- **Right to erasure (eyðingaréttur, "rétturinn til að gleymast") — GDPR Art. 17.** Also known as the right to be forgotten. You can ask us to delete personal data about you in the circumstances listed in Art. 17 — for example, where the data is no longer necessary for the purposes for which it was collected, where you withdraw consent and there is no other lawful basis, or where you object to processing and we have no overriding legitimate ground to continue.
- **Right to restriction of processing (réttur til takmörkunar á vinnslu) — GDPR Art. 18.** You can ask us to limit the use of your personal data while a question about its accuracy or our right to process it is resolved.
- **Right to data portability (réttur til flutnings persónuupplýsinga) — GDPR Art. 20.** Where we process personal data on the basis of consent or contract and by automated means, you can ask for a copy in a structured, commonly used, machine-readable format and, where technically feasible, ask us to transmit it directly to another controller.
- **Right to object (andmælaréttur) — GDPR Art. 21.** You can object to processing based on legitimate interests or the public interest at any time, including profiling on those bases. You have an absolute right to object to processing for direct marketing.
- **Rights relating to automated decision-making and profiling — GDPR Art. 22.** See Section 15.
- **Right to withdraw consent (réttur til afturköllunar á samþykki) — GDPR Art. 7(3).** Where we rely on your consent, you can withdraw it at any time with effect for the future. Withdrawal does not affect the lawfulness of processing carried out on the basis of your consent before withdrawal.
- **Right to lodge a complaint with Persónuvernd (réttur til að leggja fram kvörtun til Persónuverndar) — GDPR Art. 77; Act 90/2018 Art. 39.** You can complain to the Icelandic Data Protection Authority at any time. See Section 17 for contact details.

### 14.1 How to exercise your rights

Send a request to {{Company Email Privacy}}, or use any in-product mechanism we make available (for example, an in-app data export or account-deletion tool).

We will:

1. Acknowledge your request promptly.
2. Verify your identity using information we already hold, to make sure we do not disclose data to the wrong person. We will not request more information than we need to verify your identity.
3. Respond within **one month** of receipt of the request, in line with GDPR Art. 12(3). Where the request is complex or where we have received a number of requests, we may extend the response window by up to **two further months** and will notify you within the original month if we do so.
4. Tell you, where we cannot fulfil all or part of your request, the reason and any further steps you can take, including the right to complain to Persónuvernd or to seek a judicial remedy.

You can ask someone to make a request on your behalf. We may ask for evidence of their authority (for example, a written authorisation or a power of attorney) before we act.

You will not be charged a fee for exercising these rights, except in the narrow cases the law permits — where requests are **manifestly unfounded or excessive**, in which case we may charge a reasonable fee or refuse to act on the request, in line with GDPR Art. 12(5).

If you are not satisfied with how we have handled your request, see Section 17 for how to complain to Persónuvernd or seek a judicial remedy.

## 15. Automated decision-making & profiling

We do not currently use automated decision-making that produces legal or similarly significant effects on you within the meaning of **GDPR Art. 22**.

We do use automated processing for:

- Spam and abuse detection.
- Security and fraud signals (for example, detecting suspicious sign-in attempts).
- Service-improvement analytics described in Section 6.
- *OPTIONAL — only if you do this.* Personalising content or recommendations within the Service, where the personalisation does not produce legal or similarly significant effects.

Where any automated process could, in a particular case, qualify as automated decision-making with legal or similarly significant effects within the meaning of Art. 22, we will:

- tell you about it at the point of collection;
- give you a way to ask for human intervention, to express your point of view, and to contest the decision;
- explain the logic involved at a level you can understand, the significance of the processing, and the envisaged consequences for you.

If you have questions about a specific automated process, write to {{Company Email Privacy}}.
