# Data Processing Agreement — EU/EEA Variant

This Data Processing Agreement (the "DPA") is entered into between `{{Controller Legal Name}}`, with registered seat at `{{Controller Address}}` ("Controller"), and `{{Processor Legal Name}}`, with registered seat at `{{Processor Address}}` ("Processor"), together the "Parties" and each a "Party". It supplements and forms part of `{{Underlying Agreement Name}}` dated `{{Underlying Agreement Date}}` (the "Principal Agreement") under which Processor provides `{{Description of Services}}` (the "Services") to Controller. This DPA takes effect on `{{DPA Effective Date}}`.

---

## 1. Plain-language summary

In short: this contract sets the rules for how Processor handles personal data on Controller's behalf so that both parties meet Article 28 of the General Data Protection Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2016/679, "GDPR"), the German Federal Data Protection Act ("BDSG"), and — where relevant — the revised Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection ("FADP"). Controller decides why and how personal data is processed. Processor follows Controller's documented instructions, keeps the data confidential and secure, helps Controller respond to data subjects and regulators, controls onward transfers and sub-processors, and returns or deletes the data when the engagement ends.

This DPA also incorporates the European Commission's Standard Contractual Clauses adopted by Implementing Decision (EU) 2021/914 of 4 June 2021 (the "SCCs") for any transfer of personal data to a third country that lacks an adequacy decision under Article 45 GDPR, with module selection and annex content specified in **Annex 4**.

This DPA is a contract, not legal advice. Each Party should obtain its own counsel before signing.

## 2. Definitions

In short: defined terms below mirror GDPR vocabulary. Where a term is not defined here, it has the meaning given in the GDPR or the Principal Agreement.

2.1 **"Applicable Data Protection Law"** means, collectively and as applicable to the processing under this DPA: (a) the GDPR; (b) the German BDSG and the Telecommunications-Telemedia Data Protection Act ("TTDSG"); (c) the revised Swiss FADP and its implementing ordinance; and (d) any binding guidance, decision, or code of conduct issued by a competent supervisory authority that has the force of law.

2.2 **"Controller", "Processor", "Personal Data", "Processing", "Data Subject", "Special Categories of Personal Data", "Personal Data Breach", "Sub-processor", "Supervisory Authority", and "Third Country"** have the meanings given in Article 4 GDPR (and, where applicable, Article 5 FADP).

2.3 **"Authorised Sub-processor"** means a Sub-processor approved under Section 9 and listed in **Annex 3**.

2.4 **"EEA"** means the European Economic Area.

2.5 **"Restricted Transfer"** means a transfer of Personal Data from the EEA, the United Kingdom, or Switzerland to a Third Country that is not the subject of a current adequacy decision applicable to the recipient.

2.6 **"SCCs"** means the Standard Contractual Clauses set out in the Annex to Commission Implementing Decision (EU) 2021/914.

2.7 **"Services"** means the services described in the Principal Agreement, as scoped for this DPA in **Annex 1**.

2.8 **"TIA"** means a Transfer Impact Assessment of the kind required by the European Data Protection Board ("EDPB") Recommendations 01/2020 on supplementary measures, version 2.0.

2.9 **"TOMs"** means the technical and organisational measures described in **Annex 2**, as required by Article 32 GDPR.

## 3. Subject matter, duration, nature and purpose of processing

In short: this section names what is being processed, why, and for how long, as required by Article 28(3) GDPR.

3.1 **Subject matter.** Processing of Personal Data by Processor in the course of providing the Services to Controller, as further described in **Annex 1**.

3.2 **Duration.** From the DPA Effective Date until the later of (a) termination or expiry of the Principal Agreement and (b) the date Processor returns or deletes all Personal Data under Section 16. Backup and legal-hold tail periods set out in Section 16 survive that date.

3.3 **Nature of processing.** Hosting, transmission, storage, retrieval, structuring, indexing, analysis, troubleshooting, support, deletion, and any related operations described in **Annex 1**.

3.4 **Purpose.** To enable Processor to provide the Services and to fulfil Processor's obligations under the Principal Agreement and this DPA. Processor will not process Personal Data for any other purpose, including its own commercial purposes, except where Article 28(3)(a) GDPR permits processing required by Union or Member State law to which Processor is subject (in which case Processor will inform Controller of that legal requirement before processing, unless that law prohibits such notice on important grounds of public interest).

## 5. Roles and responsibilities

In short: Controller is the controller; Processor is the processor. This is a true Article 28 relationship, not a joint-controllership.

5.1 **Recital — Article 28(3).** The Parties acknowledge and agree that, with respect to the processing of Personal Data under this DPA, Controller is the "controller" and Processor is the "processor" within the meaning of Article 4 GDPR. Where a separate engagement could give rise to joint-controllership under Article 26 GDPR, that engagement is out of scope of this DPA and must be governed by a separate joint-controller arrangement.

5.2 **Independent controllers.** Each Party remains an independent controller for personal data it processes for its own administrative purposes (e.g., Processor's processing of Controller-side billing-contact data for invoicing). Such independent processing is not governed by this DPA.

5.3 **Processor undertaking.** Processor will process Personal Data only in accordance with this DPA, the documented instructions of Controller (Section 6), and Applicable Data Protection Law. Processor will provide all reasonable cooperation needed for Controller to demonstrate compliance with Article 28 GDPR.

5.4 **Controller undertaking.** Controller warrants that it has a valid lawful basis under Article 6 GDPR (and, where applicable, Article 9 GDPR) for the processing it instructs, that it has provided any required Article 13/14 information to Data Subjects, and that the instructions it gives Processor comply with Applicable Data Protection Law.

## 6. Documented processing instructions

In short: Processor follows Controller's written instructions and pushes back if an instruction looks unlawful, as required by Article 28(3)(a) and (3) ¶2 GDPR.

6.1 **Scope of instructions.** Controller's documented instructions to Processor consist of (a) this DPA (including the Annexes), (b) the Principal Agreement, (c) the configuration choices Controller makes in the Services, and (d) any further written instructions Controller issues from time to time consistent with the Services. Verbal instructions are not binding unless confirmed in writing within `{{Verbal Instruction Confirmation Window}}` (default seven (7) days).

6.2 **Restriction.** Processor will not process Personal Data outside Controller's instructions unless required by Union or Member State law to which Processor is subject. Where so required, Processor will inform Controller of the legal requirement before processing, unless that law prohibits notice on important grounds of public interest.

6.3 **Notification of unlawful instruction.** Pursuant to Article 28(3) ¶2 GDPR, if Processor reasonably considers that an instruction infringes the GDPR or other Applicable Data Protection Law, Processor will inform Controller without undue delay and may suspend execution of that instruction until Controller confirms or amends it in writing. Processor's reasonable concerns will be set out in writing with sufficient specificity to allow Controller to assess them.

6.4 **Out-of-scope requests.** *OPTIONAL — only if Processor offers professional-services consulting that goes beyond the Services.* Any work outside the documented Services scope must be commissioned through a separate written instruction or change order and is outside this DPA's scope until so commissioned.

## 7. Confidentiality of personnel

In short: people on Processor's side who handle the data must be sworn to confidentiality, per Article 28(3)(b) GDPR.

7.1 **Confidentiality undertakings.** Processor will ensure that any natural person acting under its authority, including its employees, contractors, and agents, who has access to Personal Data is bound by a written contractual or statutory confidentiality obligation that survives termination of their engagement and that is at least as protective as the confidentiality obligations in the Principal Agreement.

7.2 **Need-to-know basis.** Access to Personal Data is restricted to personnel who need access to perform the Services. Processor will maintain a process for revoking access promptly upon role change or departure.

7.3 **Training.** Processor will provide regular data protection and information security training to personnel with access to Personal Data, and will keep records of completion for the duration of this DPA.

## 8. Security measures (TOMs)

In short: Processor implements appropriate technical and organisational measures under Article 32 GDPR. Specifics live in **Annex 2**.

8.1 **Article 32 enumeration.** Taking into account the state of the art, the costs of implementation, and the nature, scope, context and purposes of processing, as well as the risks to Data Subjects' rights and freedoms, Processor will implement and maintain appropriate technical and organisational measures including, where appropriate:

(a) the **pseudonymisation and encryption** of Personal Data, in transit and at rest, in accordance with **Annex 2**;

(b) the ability to ensure the ongoing **confidentiality, integrity, availability and resilience** of processing systems and services;

(c) the ability to **restore** the availability of and access to Personal Data in a timely manner in the event of a physical or technical incident; and

(d) a process for **regularly testing, assessing and evaluating** the effectiveness of those technical and organisational measures.

8.2 **Annex 2.** The current TOMs are described in **Annex 2**. Processor may update TOMs from time to time, provided that the updated TOMs do not materially diminish the level of protection. Material reductions require Controller's prior written consent.

8.3 **Risk-proportionate controls.** Processor will calibrate measures to the risks presented by the processing, including risks of accidental or unlawful destruction, loss, alteration, unauthorised disclosure of, or access to, Personal Data.

8.4 **Special categories.** *OPTIONAL — only if Annex 1 indicates Article 9 data.* Where the Services involve Special Categories of Personal Data, Processor will apply the additional safeguards in **Annex 2** Section "Special Categories", including stricter access controls, segregated key management, and enhanced audit logging.

8.5 **Compliance with codes and certifications.** *OPTIONAL — only if Processor is certified.* Processor's adherence to `{{Certifications, e.g., ISO/IEC 27001:2022, ISO/IEC 27701, SOC 2 Type II, BSI C5}}` is one means of demonstrating compliance with this Section but does not limit Processor's underlying obligations.

## 9. Sub-processors

In short: Processor may use sub-processors only with Controller's authorisation, must flow this DPA's terms down to them, and remains liable for them, as required by Article 28(2) and (4) GDPR.

9.1 **General authorisation.** Controller grants Processor general written authorisation to engage Sub-processors, subject to the conditions in this Section. The current list is in **Annex 3**.

9.2 **Notice of changes.** Processor will notify Controller of any intended addition or replacement of Sub-processors at least `{{Notification Window}}` (default thirty (30)) days before the Sub-processor begins processing Personal Data, by `{{Notification Mechanism, e.g., email to Controller's DPO contact and update to subprocessor page}}`.

9.3 **Right to object.** Within the Notification Window, Controller may object on reasonable data-protection grounds. The Parties will discuss the objection in good faith. If the Parties cannot agree on a resolution, Controller may terminate the affected portion of the Services without penalty by written notice within thirty (30) days after the end of the Notification Window; Processor will refund pre-paid fees for the terminated, undelivered portion.

9.4 **Specific authorisation alternative.** *OPTIONAL — selected via merge field `{{Sub-processor Authorisation Mode}}` = "specific".* Where Controller selects specific authorisation, Processor will not engage any new Sub-processor without Controller's prior written consent for that Sub-processor. Annex 3 will list pre-approved Sub-processors as of the DPA Effective Date.

9.5 **Flow-down.** Processor will impose on each Sub-processor, by way of a written contract, data-protection obligations that are no less protective than those imposed on Processor under this DPA, in particular providing sufficient guarantees to implement appropriate technical and organisational measures so that the processing meets the requirements of the GDPR. Where the Sub-processor fails to fulfil its data protection obligations, Processor remains fully liable to Controller for the performance of that Sub-processor's obligations, in accordance with Article 28(4) GDPR.

9.6 **Sub-processor cross-border transfers.** Where a Sub-processor is established in or processes Personal Data from a Third Country, Section 14 and **Annex 4** apply, and Processor will execute the appropriate SCCs Module (typically Module 3) with the Sub-processor.

9.7 **List on request.** Processor will, upon Controller's reasonable request, provide a current list of Authorised Sub-processors with the information required by **Annex 3**.

## 10. Data subject rights assistance

In short: Processor helps Controller answer Data Subjects exercising their rights, per Article 28(3)(e) GDPR.

10.1 **Assistance.** Taking into account the nature of the processing, Processor will assist Controller by appropriate technical and organisational measures, insofar as possible, for the fulfilment of Controller's obligation to respond to requests for the exercise of Data Subject rights under Chapter III of the GDPR, including the rights of access (Art. 15), rectification (Art. 16), erasure (Art. 17), restriction of processing (Art. 18), data portability (Art. 20), objection (Art. 21), and rights related to automated decision-making (Art. 22).

10.2 **Direct requests.** If Processor receives a Data Subject request directly, Processor will (a) not respond to the request other than to acknowledge receipt and direct the Data Subject to Controller, unless legally required or instructed by Controller to do so, and (b) forward the request to Controller without undue delay and, in any event, within `{{DSR Forwarding SLA}}` (default three (3)) business days.

10.3 **Tools and self-service.** Where the Services include self-service tools allowing Controller to fulfil Data Subject requests (e.g., export, deletion, account-level redaction), Controller will use those tools first; Processor will provide additional assistance to the extent self-service tools are insufficient.

10.4 **Costs.** Reasonable assistance is included in the Services. Materially disproportionate or extraordinary requests may be charged at Processor's then-current professional-services rates, with prior written notice to Controller.

## 11. Assistance with controller compliance

In short: beyond Data Subject rights, Processor helps Controller meet its broader GDPR duties — security, breach notification, DPIAs, prior consultation, per Article 28(3)(f).

11.1 **Article 32 — security assistance.** Processor will assist Controller in ensuring compliance with Article 32 GDPR by maintaining the TOMs in **Annex 2** and providing information reasonably necessary to demonstrate compliance, including security questionnaires, penetration-test summaries, and audit reports under Section 15.

11.2 **Articles 33–34 — breach assistance.** Processor will assist Controller in complying with notification obligations to the Supervisory Authority (Article 33) and Data Subjects (Article 34) by providing the information described in Section 12 in a manner and timeframe that allows Controller to meet its statutory deadlines.

11.3 **Article 35 — DPIAs.** Where Controller is required to carry out a Data Protection Impact Assessment in respect of the Services, Processor will provide reasonable assistance, including information about the nature, scope, context, and purposes of the processing it performs, the TOMs it applies, the Sub-processors it uses, and the locations of processing.

11.4 **Article 36 — prior consultation.** Where Controller, on the basis of the DPIA, is required to consult the Supervisory Authority before processing under Article 36 GDPR, Processor will provide reasonable assistance, including by making available information about the processing it performs.

11.5 **Costs.** Article 28 assistance is included in the fees for the Services. Where assistance becomes materially disproportionate to the contracted scope (for example, repeated bespoke DPIAs or prolonged regulator engagement), Processor may charge professional-services rates with prior written notice.

## 12. Personal data breach notification

In short: if Processor learns of a Personal Data Breach, it tells Controller fast — fast enough that Controller can meet the GDPR's 72-hour deadline.

12.1 **Notification trigger.** Processor will notify Controller of a Personal Data Breach affecting Personal Data processed under this DPA without undue delay after becoming aware of it, and in any event within `{{Breach Notification SLA}}` (default forty-eight (48)) hours of becoming aware. "Becoming aware" follows the standard articulated in EDPB Guidelines 9/2022 on personal data breach notification under GDPR.

12.2 **Notification content.** To the extent known and as it becomes available, the notification will include:

(a) a description of the nature of the breach, including, where possible, the categories and approximate number of Data Subjects and Personal Data records concerned;

(b) the name and contact details of Processor's Data Protection Officer or other contact point where further information can be obtained;

(c) a description of the likely consequences of the breach; and

(d) a description of the measures taken or proposed to address the breach and to mitigate its possible adverse effects.

12.3 **Rolling updates.** Where the information cannot be provided at the same time, it may be provided in phases without further undue delay. Processor will continue to update Controller until the incident is resolved.

12.4 **Cooperation.** Processor will cooperate with Controller's reasonable instructions to investigate, contain, remediate, and document the breach, including providing forensic logs and root-cause analysis.

12.5 **Direct notification.** Processor will not notify Data Subjects or Supervisory Authorities of a Personal Data Breach in respect of Controller's processing without Controller's prior written consent, except where Processor is itself the controller for the affected data or is required by law to do so.

12.6 **Notification channel.** Notifications will be sent to `{{Controller Breach Contact}}` via `{{Notification Channel}}` (default secure email plus in-product banner where available).

## 13. Records of processing

In short: Processor keeps Article 30(2) records and shares them with regulators on request, with Controller's involvement.

13.1 **Article 30(2) records.** Processor will maintain a record of all categories of processing activities carried out on behalf of each controller, containing the information required by Article 30(2) GDPR (including name and contact details of Processor and any joint-processor representative, categories of processing carried out on behalf of each controller, transfers to third countries with appropriate safeguards, and a general description of the TOMs).

13.2 **Disclosure to authorities.** Processor will make those records available to the competent Supervisory Authority on request, in accordance with Article 30(4) GDPR. Where legally permitted, Processor will inform Controller before responding.

13.3 **Disclosure to Controller.** Upon Controller's reasonable written request, Processor will provide Controller with the entries in its Article 30(2) record relating to processing for Controller, redacted to remove confidential information about other customers.

## 14. International data transfers

In short: this section governs how Personal Data may leave the EEA, the UK, or Switzerland. **Annex 4** is the SCCs annex builder for transfers to a Third Country without an adequacy decision.

14.1 **General rule.** Processor and its Authorised Sub-processors will not transfer Personal Data outside the EEA, the United Kingdom, or Switzerland unless an appropriate transfer mechanism under Chapter V GDPR (or, for Switzerland, Articles 16–18 FADP) is in place.

14.2 **Adequacy decisions (Article 45 GDPR).** Personal Data may be transferred to a Third Country covered by a current adequacy decision adopted by the European Commission under Article 45 GDPR. As of the date of this DPA, those adequacy decisions cover the jurisdictions listed by the European Commission at https://commission.europa.eu/law/law-topic/data-protection/international-dimension-data-protection/adequacy-decisions_en, including the EU-US Data Privacy Framework ("EU-US DPF") for certified U.S. organisations. The list is updated periodically; Parties will rely on the list in force at the time of transfer. `[VERIFY]`

14.3 **Standard Contractual Clauses (Article 46(2)(c)).** Where no adequacy decision applies, the Parties incorporate the SCCs by reference. The applicable Module(s), party roles, optional clauses, and annex content are specified in **Annex 4**:

(a) **Module 1** — Controller-to-Controller transfers (e.g., Processor's Controller acts as data exporter to a Controller-importer affiliate; not typical between the Parties);

(b) **Module 2** — Controller-to-Processor transfers (the most common configuration where Controller is in the EEA and Processor is in a Third Country);

(c) **Module 3** — Processor-to-Processor transfers (where Processor in the EEA acts as exporter to a Sub-processor in a Third Country); and

(d) **Module 4** — Processor-to-Controller transfers (rare reverse flow).

14.4 **Transfer Impact Assessment (Schrems II).** Before relying on the SCCs for a Restricted Transfer, the data exporter will conduct a Transfer Impact Assessment in accordance with EDPB Recommendations 01/2020 on supplementary measures, version 2.0, taking into account the specific circumstances of the transfer, the laws and practices of the destination country, and any supplementary technical, organisational, and contractual measures. Processor will provide reasonable assistance with the TIA. A placeholder TIA workflow is described in **Annex 4** Section 4.9; Controllers using the broader Legalcode workflow should consult the `legalcode-cross-border-transfer-assessment` skill. `[VERIFY]`

14.5 **Binding Corporate Rules (Article 47).** *OPTIONAL — only if applicable.* Where a Party has approved BCRs covering the transfer, the BCRs may serve as the appropriate safeguard in lieu of the SCCs, provided they are in force and applicable to the relevant entities and processing.

14.6 **Article 49 derogations.** Reliance on Article 49 derogations is a last resort, on a case-by-case basis, and only where no other Chapter V transfer mechanism applies. Processor will not rely on Article 49 derogations without Controller's prior written consent.

14.7 **Onward transfers.** Authorised Sub-processors located in Third Countries are subject to the same Chapter V requirements. Processor will execute the relevant SCCs (typically Module 3) with each such Sub-processor and reflect the transfer mechanism in **Annex 3**.

14.8 **UK and Switzerland.** For transfers from the United Kingdom, the Parties will use the UK International Data Transfer Addendum to the EU SCCs (or the UK International Data Transfer Agreement) issued by the UK Information Commissioner's Office. For transfers from Switzerland, the FDPIC Swiss adaptation of the SCCs applies, as further described in **Annex 4** Section 4.10 and the Switzerland Appendix in Section 21.

14.9 **Government access requests.** Processor will, to the extent permitted by law, (a) review the legality of any government request for Personal Data, (b) challenge requests that appear unlawful, excessive, or that are not narrowly tailored, (c) provide only the minimum data required, and (d) notify Controller of the request. Processor will publish a transparency report at least `{{Transparency Report Cadence, default annually}}` summarising government access requests.

## 15. Audits and inspections

In short: Controller can verify Processor's compliance, with audit reports usually doing the work and on-site visits reserved for cause, per Article 28(3)(h) GDPR.

15.1 **Audit right.** Controller may audit Processor's compliance with this DPA, either itself or through a mandated third-party auditor that is not a competitor of Processor and that is bound by appropriate confidentiality obligations.

15.2 **Routine satisfaction.** Processor will satisfy the audit obligation in the ordinary course by providing Controller with current third-party audit and certification reports, including (as available) `{{Routine Audit Artefacts, default SOC 2 Type II, ISO/IEC 27001:2022, ISO/IEC 27701, BSI C5, EU Cloud CoC adherence statement}}`. Controller agrees to accept these as primary evidence of compliance.

15.3 **On-site or for-cause audits.** Where (a) those reports are insufficient to demonstrate compliance with a specific obligation, (b) a Personal Data Breach has occurred, or (c) a Supervisory Authority requires a more granular review, Controller may request an on-site audit on at least `{{Audit Notice Period}}` (default thirty (30)) days' written notice, no more than once in any rolling 12-month period (regulator-driven audits and breach-driven audits do not count toward this cap). Audits will take place during business hours, will not unreasonably interfere with Processor's operations, and will respect Processor's security and confidentiality requirements.

15.4 **Costs.** Each Party bears its own costs of routine audits. Processor's reasonable on-site facilitation costs may be charged for second and subsequent on-site audits in any 12-month period; the first on-site audit per 12-month period is included.

15.5 **Supervisory Authority cooperation.** Processor will cooperate with, and contribute to, any audit, investigation, or inspection conducted by a competent Supervisory Authority in respect of Personal Data processed under this DPA.

## 16. Return or deletion on termination

In short: when the engagement ends, Controller chooses return or deletion, and Processor purges its copies, subject to a narrow backup carve-out, per Article 28(3)(g) GDPR.

16.1 **Controller's choice.** On termination or expiry of the Principal Agreement, or otherwise on Controller's written instruction, Processor will, at Controller's choice, return or delete all Personal Data processed on Controller's behalf. The default action, in the absence of a Controller instruction, is `{{Default Termination Action, "deletion"}}`.

16.2 **Return mechanism.** *OPTIONAL — only if Controller selects return.* Processor will return Personal Data via `{{Return Mechanism, default secure download in industry-standard formats}}` within `{{Return Window, default thirty (30)}}` days of the request.

16.3 **Deletion certification.** Processor will delete existing copies of Personal Data and provide Controller with a written certification of deletion within `{{Deletion Window, default sixty (60)}}` days of the request, except for copies retained as required by Section 16.4.

16.4 **Backup carve-out.** Processor may retain Personal Data in routine encrypted backups beyond the deletion window, provided that (a) such Personal Data is not actively processed, (b) access is strictly limited to personnel with a legitimate operational need, (c) the data is deleted in the next routine backup cycle and no later than `{{Backup Retention Cap, default ninety (90)}}` days, and (d) the obligations of this DPA continue to apply to the retained copies until deletion.

16.5 **Statutory retention.** Where Union or Member State law requires Processor to retain Personal Data, Processor will inform Controller of the requirement and the retention period and will continue to apply the protections of this DPA to the retained data for the remainder of that period.

## 17. Liability and indemnity

In short: liability follows the Principal Agreement and the SCCs; statutory and Data Subject claims are not limited where the GDPR forbids it.

17.1 **Caps and exclusions.** Liability under this DPA is subject to the limitation-of-liability and exclusion-of-damages provisions of the Principal Agreement, except to the extent those provisions would limit (a) liability owed to a Data Subject under Article 82 GDPR, (b) administrative fines imposed under Article 83 GDPR (each Party bears its own fines), or (c) other liabilities that cannot lawfully be limited.

17.2 **Inter-party allocation.** Where one Party pays compensation to a Data Subject under Article 82(4) GDPR for damage caused by processing for which both Parties are responsible, the paying Party may claim back from the other Party the part of the compensation corresponding to that other Party's share of responsibility under Article 82(5) GDPR.

17.3 **Indemnity.** *OPTIONAL — only if the Principal Agreement does not already address indemnification for data-protection claims.* Each Party will indemnify the other against direct losses incurred as a result of the indemnifying Party's material breach of this DPA, subject to the caps in Section 17.1 and to the indemnification mechanics in the Principal Agreement (including notice, control of defence, and cooperation).

17.4 **SCCs liability.** Where the SCCs apply to a Restricted Transfer, the liability provisions in the relevant SCCs Module prevail over this Section to the extent of any conflict, in accordance with the order of precedence in Section 19.

## 19. Order of precedence and miscellaneous

In short: where documents conflict, the SCCs win for transfers; this DPA wins over the Principal Agreement on data-protection topics; jurisdiction-specific appendices win over general DPA terms within their scope.

19.1 **Order of precedence.** In the event of conflict, the documents apply in the following order, from highest to lowest:

(a) the SCCs and any other Chapter V transfer mechanism, with respect to the matters they cover;

(b) the Germany Appendix (Section 20) and the Switzerland Appendix (Section 21), each within its territorial and topical scope;

(c) this DPA (including Annexes);

(d) the Principal Agreement.

19.2 **Severability.** If any provision of this DPA is held invalid or unenforceable, the remaining provisions remain in full force and effect, and the Parties will negotiate in good faith to replace the affected provision with a valid one that gives effect to the original intent.

19.3 **Variation.** Variations to this DPA must be in writing and signed by authorised representatives of both Parties. Where Applicable Data Protection Law changes in a way that requires an update, the Parties will negotiate amendments in good faith.

19.4 **Notices.** Notices under this DPA will be given in writing in accordance with the Principal Agreement's notice provisions, with copies to `{{Controller DPO Contact}}` and `{{Processor DPO Contact}}`.

19.5 **Counterparts and electronic signature.** This DPA may be executed in counterparts and by electronic signature, each of which is an original and all of which together constitute the same instrument.

19.6 **Entire agreement on data protection.** This DPA, together with the Principal Agreement and any executed SCCs, constitutes the entire agreement between the Parties relating to processing of Personal Data and supersedes any prior data-processing terms in respect of the Services.

19.7 **No third-party beneficiaries except Data Subjects.** This DPA does not create third-party-beneficiary rights, except that Data Subjects have third-party-beneficiary rights to the extent provided by the GDPR and by the relevant SCCs Module (where executed).

## 20. Germany appendix (DE-DSGVO)

In short: where Controller or Processor is in Germany, or where the BDSG or TTDSG applies to the processing, this Appendix supplements the DPA. Within its scope, it prevails over conflicting general terms.

20.1 **Scope.** This Appendix applies where (a) the Personal Data relates to Data Subjects in Germany, (b) Controller or Processor is established in Germany, or (c) the processing is otherwise subject to the BDSG or TTDSG.

20.2 **Section 22 BDSG — special-category processing.** Where the processing involves special categories of personal data within the meaning of Article 9 GDPR, the Parties will apply the additional appropriate and specific measures set out in §22(2) BDSG, including, as relevant, technical and organisational measures to ensure compliance with Article 5 GDPR, awareness-raising measures, restrictions on access, pseudonymisation, encryption, logging of access, and procedures for the regular review of necessity. Specific measures are reflected in **Annex 2** Section "Special Categories".

20.3 **Section 26 BDSG / new §§ for employee data.** *OPTIONAL — only if employee data is in scope.* Where the processing concerns employee Personal Data, Processor acknowledges the BDSG provisions on employee data protection (currently §26 BDSG, subject to legislative reform `[VERIFY]`), including the principles of necessity and proportionality, the prohibition on covert monitoring outside narrowly defined cases, and the obligations on consent in employment contexts. Controller is responsible for the lawful basis (typically §26 BDSG or a collective agreement) and for any required works council co-determination under §87(1)(6) Betriebsverfassungsgesetz ("BetrVG"). Processor will provide the technical capability to implement Controller's instructions arising from co-determination outcomes.

20.4 **Section 38 BDSG — DPO threshold.** Controller and Processor each acknowledge the BDSG threshold for the mandatory designation of a Data Protection Officer (currently, organisations with at least twenty (20) persons constantly engaged in the automated processing of personal data, in addition to Article 37 GDPR triggers). `[VERIFY]` Each Party that meets the threshold will designate a DPO and provide the DPO's contact details to the other Party.

20.5 **Section 25 TTDSG — terminal-equipment storage and access.** *OPTIONAL — only if the Services involve client-side storage or access (cookies, local storage, SDKs, pixels) on Data Subjects' devices.* Controller is responsible for obtaining consent or relying on the strict-necessity exemption under §25 TTDSG before any storage or access on a Data Subject's terminal equipment by means of the Services. Processor will provide the technical capability to support Controller's consent regime, including consent-mode signalling and (where the Services include such tooling) consent management.

20.6 **Works council interplay (BetrVG §87(1)(6)).** Where the Services include any technical means suitable for monitoring employee performance or behaviour, Controller is responsible for fulfilling co-determination obligations under §87(1)(6) BetrVG before deployment. Processor will reasonably support Controller in providing required information to the works council, subject to confidentiality.

20.7 **Supervisory authority navigation — BfDI vs. Land-LDA.** Each Party acknowledges that, in Germany, supervisory competence is split:

(a) the Federal Commissioner for Data Protection and Freedom of Information ("BfDI") is competent for federal public bodies and for telecommunications and postal service providers; and

(b) the sixteen Land Data Protection Authorities ("Land-LDA") are competent for private-sector controllers and processors and for state-level public bodies, with competence following the controller's or processor's place of establishment under §40 BDSG. Cross-Land processing is coordinated through the Datenschutzkonferenz ("DSK").

For private-sector engagements under this DPA, the relevant Land-LDA is `{{Land-LDA, default determined by Processor's main establishment in Germany}}`. `[VERIFY]`

20.8 **Bookkeeping and tax retention.** Where the Services involve documents or data that are subject to German bookkeeping retention rules, the retention period applicable under §147 Abgabenordnung ("AO") and §257 Handelsgesetzbuch ("HGB") (currently between six (6) and ten (10) years depending on the document type) `[VERIFY]` overrides the deletion obligations under Section 16 of this DPA for the affected data, and Processor will continue to apply this DPA's protections during the statutory retention.

20.9 **Language.** *OPTIONAL.* The Parties may execute a German-language version of this DPA. In the event of conflict between language versions, `{{Controlling Language, default English}}` controls.

## 21. Switzerland appendix (CH-FDPIC)

In short: where the revised FADP applies, this Appendix adapts the DPA. Within its scope, it prevails over conflicting general terms.

21.1 **Scope.** This Appendix applies where (a) Personal Data relating to Data Subjects in Switzerland is processed under this DPA, or (b) Controller or Processor is established in Switzerland and the FADP applies.

21.2 **FADP Article 9 — processor delegation.** The Parties acknowledge that Article 9 FADP imposes obligations on Controller (the "controller" within the meaning of the FADP) when delegating processing to a third party, including ensuring the third party is able to guarantee data security and processes data only as the controller would be entitled to. The substantive obligations of this DPA satisfy those FADP requirements; in case of any gap, the Parties will read this DPA to provide protections at least equivalent to those required by the FADP.

21.3 **Swiss adaptation of the SCCs.** Where the SCCs are incorporated under Section 14 and **Annex 4** for transfers of Personal Data subject to the FADP, the following adaptations apply, in line with FDPIC guidance:

(a) references to the "GDPR" are read, for purposes of FADP transfers, as references to the FADP;

(b) references to "Member State" or to "EU law" are read as references to "Switzerland" or "Swiss law", as the case may be;

(c) references to the "competent supervisory authority" include the FDPIC;

(d) Clause 13 of the SCCs is read so that the FDPIC is the competent supervisory authority for FADP-only transfers, while the lead EU Supervisory Authority remains competent for joint EU/Swiss transfers; and

(e) until the revised FADP narrowed the scope, the SCCs were extended to apply to legal-entity data; under the revised FADP the personal scope is closer to GDPR, and the Parties will read the SCCs consistently with the revised FADP. `[VERIFY]`

21.4 **Cross-border transfers from Switzerland.** Transfers of Personal Data from Switzerland to a third country require an adequate level of protection or another transfer mechanism recognised under Articles 16–17 FADP. The Parties will rely on (a) the FDPIC's list of states with adequate protection, (b) the FDPIC-adapted SCCs (per Section 21.3), (c) BCRs recognised by the FDPIC, or (d) where applicable, the Swiss-US DPF for Swiss data transferred to certified U.S. organisations. `[VERIFY]`

21.5 **FDPIC as supervisory authority.** The competent supervisory authority for processing subject to the FADP is the Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner ("FDPIC"). Each Party will cooperate with the FDPIC as required.

21.6 **DPIA equivalent.** The FADP requires a Data Protection Impact Assessment in defined high-risk situations (Article 22 FADP). Section 11.3 applies, with references to GDPR Article 35 read as references to FADP Article 22.

21.7 **Breach notification under FADP.** Article 24 FADP requires the controller to notify the FDPIC of breaches likely to result in a high risk to data subjects' personality or fundamental rights, as soon as possible. Section 12 applies, and Processor will provide the equivalent assistance for FADP notifications.

## Annex 4 — SCCs Module Builder

In short: this Annex selects and configures the European Commission's Standard Contractual Clauses (Implementing Decision (EU) 2021/914) for any Restricted Transfer under Section 14. Operative SCCs are incorporated by reference; this Annex chooses Modules, captures Annex I/II/III content, and resolves optional clauses.

### 4.1 Module selection guide

**Step 1 — Identify the data exporter and importer for each transfer flow.** A "flow" is a discrete movement of Personal Data from one party in the EEA, UK, or Switzerland to another party in a Third Country.

**Step 2 — Determine each party's role for the flow.**

| Exporter role | Importer role | Module | Typical fact pattern |
|---|---|---|---|
| Controller | Controller | **Module 1** | Two controllers exchanging data (e.g., HR data shared between an EEA parent and a non-EEA affiliate as independent controllers). |
| Controller | Processor | **Module 2** | Most common: EEA Controller engages a non-EEA Processor (e.g., SaaS in the US). |
| Processor | Sub-processor | **Module 3** | EEA Processor engages a non-EEA Sub-processor; flow-down from the Controller-Processor relationship. |
| Processor | Controller | **Module 4** | Reverse flow: a non-EEA Controller receives data back from an EEA Processor (e.g., processor is an EEA-based service of a non-EEA controller, returning processed output). |

- [ ] Module 3 (P2P)

**Step 4 — Confirm transfer mechanism eligibility.** The Parties confirm there is no adequacy decision applicable to the importer (otherwise rely on Article 45) and that no derogation under Article 49 is being relied on.

### 4.2 Module 1 — Controller to controller

**When applicable.** Where the importer determines the purposes and means of the processing on its own account. Each party is a controller for the flow.

**Key Module-specific clauses (Implementing Decision (EU) 2021/914 SCCs).** Refer to the operative SCC text (incorporated by reference) for:

- **Clause 8.1** — Purpose limitation: importer processes the personal data only for the specific purposes set out in Annex I.B.
- **Clause 8.2** — Transparency: importer informs data subjects (with limited Article 14(5) exceptions).
- **Clause 8.3** — Accuracy and data minimisation.
- **Clause 8.4** — Storage limitation: importer ensures data is not kept longer than necessary; deletion at the end of the retention period.
- **Clause 8.5** — Security of processing: importer implements appropriate TOMs (cross-reference **Annex 2** of this DPA).
- **Clause 8.6** — Sensitive data: additional safeguards.
- **Clause 8.7** — Onward transfers: only on the bases listed in Clause 8.7.

**Annex content for Module 1.** Annex I.A (Parties), Annex I.B (Description of transfer), and Annex II (TOMs) are reflected in **Annex 4.7** below, which cross-references Annexes 1, 2 of this DPA.

### 4.4 Module 3 — Processor to processor

**When applicable.** EEA Processor (as exporter) sends data to a non-EEA Sub-processor (as importer). The Controller is a third-party beneficiary.

**Key Module-specific features.**

- **Clause 8.1 (Module 3)** — Instructions flow down: importer processes on documented instructions from the exporter, which itself acts on the controller's instructions; importer notifies exporter (and exporter notifies controller) of any infringing instruction.
- **Clause 8.5** — Security and breach notification: importer notifies exporter without undue delay; exporter cascades to controller per Section 12 of this DPA.
- **Clause 8.8** — Onward transfers: same constraints as Module 2.
- **Clause 9** — Sub-processing of the importer: the importer may engage further sub-processors only with the controller's authorisation (cascading), and must flow down equivalent obligations.

**Use case.** Where this Processor is the exporter and a non-EEA Sub-processor is the importer (for example, hosting a region-specific component), Processor will execute Module 3 SCCs with the Sub-processor and reflect the transfer mechanism in **Annex 3**. Controller receives third-party-beneficiary rights under those SCCs.

### 4.5 Module 4 — Processor to controller

**When applicable.** EEA-based Processor returns data to a non-EEA Controller. Less common; arises where, for example, an EEA-based service processor returns data to its non-EEA customer-controller.

**Key Module-specific features.**

- The clauses are streamlined (no purpose-limitation clause analogous to Module 2) because the importer is the controller and determines purposes.
- **Clause 8.1 (Module 4)** — Importer (controller) ensures lawful basis and provides any required Data Subject information.
- **Clause 8.2** — Onward transfers from importer to further controllers/processors are constrained.
- **Clause 8.3** — Security: applies to importer in its role as controller, with TOMs in **Annex 2** read accordingly.

### 4.9 Transfer Impact Assessment (Schrems II)

Before relying on the SCCs, the data exporter must conduct a TIA, taking into account:

(a) the **specific circumstances of the transfer** — categories and purposes, number of data subjects, sensitivity, format (encrypted, pseudonymised, plaintext), onward transfers, supply chain;

(c) any **supplementary measures** — technical (strong encryption with keys held by the exporter, split processing, end-to-end encryption), contractual (transparency, audit, notice, challenge, exit), organisational (internal policies, transparency reporting, data-minimisation reviews); and

(d) **documentation** of the assessment and decision.

A TIA template is maintained by Controller and updated at least annually or upon material change (e.g., new surveillance law, court ruling, or change in importer's circumstances). For the broader Legalcode workflow, see the `legalcode-cross-border-transfer-assessment` skill.
