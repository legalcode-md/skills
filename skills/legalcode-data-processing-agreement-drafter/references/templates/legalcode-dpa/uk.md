# Data Processing Agreement — United Kingdom

This Data Processing Agreement (the "DPA") forms part of, and is subject to, the master agreement, order form, or other written contract between **{{Controller Legal Name}}** (the "Controller") and **{{Processor Legal Name}}** (the "Processor") under which the Processor provides the Services (the "Principal Agreement"). This DPA governs the Processor's processing of Personal Data on behalf of the Controller and is intended to satisfy Article 28 of the UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018. The "Effective Date" is the date of last signature where this DPA is signed as a stand-alone instrument, or otherwise the effective date of the Principal Agreement.

---

## 1. Plain-language summary

This section explains in everyday terms what the rest of the document does, so a non-lawyer reader can understand the parties' commitments before reading the formal clauses.

The Controller is the organisation that decides why and how Personal Data is processed. The Processor handles that data only to provide the Services and only on the Controller's documented instructions. This DPA sets out who does what, the security measures the Processor maintains, the rules for engaging sub-processors, how the parties handle data subject requests and personal data breaches, what happens when Personal Data leaves the United Kingdom, and how the agreement ends.

The DPA is written to comply with the United Kingdom General Data Protection Regulation (the "UK GDPR"), the Data Protection Act 2018 (the "DPA 2018"), the Privacy and Electronic Communications (EC Directive) Regulations 2003 ("PECR"), and — to the extent in force at the Effective Date — the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 ("DUAA 2025") `[VERIFY — DUAA 2025 phased commencement]`. International transfers are handled using the United Kingdom's standalone International Data Transfer Agreement ("IDTA") or the UK Addendum to the EU Standard Contractual Clauses (the "UK Addendum").

If a defined term in this DPA conflicts with one in the Principal Agreement, the DPA prevails for data-protection purposes. If a term of this DPA conflicts with the IDTA, the UK Addendum, or any binding corporate rules incorporated by reference, the transfer instrument prevails to the extent required by applicable data protection law. This DPA is read together with **Annex 1 (Description of processing)**, **Annex 2 (TOMs)**, **Annex 3 (Sub-processor list)**, and **Annex 4 (UK Transfer Mechanism Builder)**, each forming an integral part of the DPA.

## 2. Definitions

This section sets out the meaning of capitalised terms used in the DPA, drawing on UK GDPR Article 4 and section 3 of the DPA 2018 so that the parties share a common vocabulary.

Capitalised terms not defined here have the meanings given in the Principal Agreement or, failing that, in Applicable Data Protection Law.

**"Approved Addendum"** means the template "International Data Transfer Addendum to the EU Commission Standard Contractual Clauses" issued by the Information Commissioner under section 119A of the DPA 2018 and laid before Parliament, as amended or replaced from time to time.

**"Controller"**, **"Processor"**, **"Personal Data"**, **"Processing"**, **"Special Category Data"**, **"Data Subject"**, **"Personal Data Breach"**, **"Recipient"**, **"Supervisory Authority"**, and **"Third Party"** each have the meanings given in Article 4 of the UK GDPR and section 3 of the DPA 2018, save that "Controller" and "Processor" identify the parties as set out in clause 5.

**"DPA 2018"** means the Data Protection Act 2018. **"DUAA 2025"** means the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025, to the extent in force at the relevant time `[VERIFY]`. **"ICO"** means the Information Commissioner's Office, the United Kingdom's Supervisory Authority. **"IDTA"** means the Approved IDTA, completed in accordance with Annex 4. **"PECR"** means the Privacy and Electronic Communications (EC Directive) Regulations 2003. **"UK Addendum"** means the Approved Addendum, completed in accordance with Annex 4.

**"Restricted Country"** means a country, territory, sector, or international organisation not the subject of UK adequacy regulations made under section 17A of the DPA 2018. **"Restricted Transfer"** means a transfer of Personal Data from the United Kingdom (or onward from a Recipient in the United Kingdom) to a Restricted Country.

## 3. Subject matter, duration, nature & purpose of processing

This section identifies the activity that the DPA regulates so the parties can later test whether any given processing falls inside or outside the agreement.

The subject matter is the provision of the Services. The duration is the term of the Principal Agreement plus any post-termination return-or-delete period under clause 16. The nature of the processing comprises the operations on Personal Data necessary to deliver the Services — including collection, storage, retrieval, use, transmission, restriction, erasure, and destruction. The purpose is to enable the Controller to use the Services for its business purposes and to receive the contractual benefits of the Principal Agreement.

A detailed description of these elements — with the categories of Data Subjects and Personal Data, the frequency of processing, and applicable retention periods — is set out in **Annex 1** in the form required by Article 28(3) of the UK GDPR.

The Processor shall not process Personal Data for any purpose other than as set out in this DPA, the Principal Agreement, or as instructed in writing under clause 6, except where required by a law to which the Processor is subject (in which case clause 6 applies).

## 5. Roles & responsibilities

This section makes clear which party is the Controller and which is the Processor for the relevant processing activity. It maps the parties onto the statutory roles defined in Article 4 of the UK GDPR.

For processing under this DPA, the Controller is the "controller" within the meaning of Article 4(7) UK GDPR, and the Processor is the "processor" within the meaning of Article 4(8) UK GDPR, in each case acting under the requirements of Article 28(3).

The Controller shall comply with its controller obligations under Applicable Data Protection Law, including providing Article 13–14 transparency information, identifying a lawful basis, ensuring data minimisation and accuracy, conducting any required DPIA, and (where applicable) designating a UK representative under Article 27 (the **{{Controller UK Representative}}**) and a DPO under Article 37.

The Processor shall comply with its processor obligations under Applicable Data Protection Law and this DPA. Where the Processor is established outside the United Kingdom and Article 27 applies, its UK representative is the **{{Processor UK Representative}}**.

**OPTIONAL — only if either party is registered with the ICO:** the parties' ICO registration numbers are **{{ICO Registration Number}}**.

Nothing in this DPA constitutes a joint-controller arrangement under Article 26 UK GDPR. Where the Processor processes Personal Data for its own purposes — for example, billing, fraud prevention on its own services, security monitoring of its own infrastructure, aggregated or de-identified analytics, or compliance with its own legal obligations — the Processor acts as a controller for that processing, which is governed by the Processor's privacy notice and not by this DPA.

## 6. Documented processing instructions

This section explains the central rule of processor law: the Processor only acts on what the Controller tells it to do, and the Processor must speak up if those instructions appear unlawful.

The Processor shall process Personal Data only on documented instructions from the Controller, including for Restricted Transfers, unless required by a UK law to which the Processor is subject. In that case, the Processor shall inform the Controller of the legal requirement before processing, unless that law prohibits notice on important public-interest grounds. This implements Article 28(3)(a) UK GDPR.

The Controller's documented instructions are: (a) this DPA; (b) the Principal Agreement; (c) configurations selected through the Services' administrative interfaces; (d) written instructions through designated support or account channels; and (e) any further written instructions agreed by the parties. Instructions outside this scope may be refused or may attract additional fees under the Principal Agreement.

The Processor shall promptly inform the Controller in writing if, in its opinion, an instruction infringes Applicable Data Protection Law. This does not amount to a duty to monitor the Controller's compliance.

The Controller warrants that its instructions and use of the Services comply with Applicable Data Protection Law and that it has the rights, lawful bases, consents, and notices necessary for the Processor to process Personal Data as contemplated by this DPA.

## 7. Confidentiality of personnel

This section confirms that everyone who can see Personal Data on the Processor's side is under a duty to keep it private.

The Processor shall ensure that any person authorised to process Personal Data — including employees, contractors, and agency workers — is committed to confidentiality or under an appropriate statutory duty of confidence. This implements Article 28(3)(b) UK GDPR.

Access to Personal Data is granted on a need-to-know basis. Personnel receive data-protection and security training at induction and at regular intervals thereafter, and confidentiality obligations survive termination of the individual's engagement.

The Processor shall maintain records sufficient to demonstrate compliance with this clause and make summary records available to the Controller on reasonable request, redacted as necessary to protect personnel privacy and system security.

## 8. Security measures (TOMs)

This section sets out the standard the Processor must meet to keep Personal Data secure. It cross-refers to a detailed annex so that operational changes can be made without reopening the contract.

Taking into account the state of the art, costs of implementation, and the nature, scope, context, and purposes of processing, as well as the risks of varying likelihood and severity to natural persons, the Processor shall implement appropriate technical and organisational measures to ensure a level of security appropriate to the risk. This gives effect to Article 32 UK GDPR.

The measures address, as appropriate: (a) pseudonymisation and encryption; (b) ongoing confidentiality, integrity, availability, and resilience of systems and services; (c) the ability to restore availability of and access to Personal Data in a timely manner after an incident; and (d) regular testing, assessment, and evaluation of effectiveness. Detailed measures are in **Annex 2**, read with the ICO's security guidance and the NCSC's Cyber Essentials and Cyber Essentials Plus schemes. The Processor's information security management system is, or is materially aligned with, **ISO/IEC 27001:2022** as described in Annex 2.

The Processor may update Annex 2 from time to time provided that the updated measures continue to provide protection at least equivalent to that at the Effective Date. The Processor shall make a current Annex 2 available and notify the Controller of any material reduction in capability through the Services' designated communication channels.

In assessing the appropriate level of security, the parties shall in particular consider the risks of accidental or unlawful destruction, loss, alteration, or unauthorised disclosure of or access to Personal Data, and shall prioritise the protection of Special Category Data and other higher-risk data.

## 10. Data subject rights assistance

This section explains how the Processor will help when an individual asks the Controller to exercise their statutory rights, such as access, rectification, or erasure.

Taking into account the nature of the processing, the Processor shall assist the Controller, by appropriate TOMs, insofar as possible, to respond to Data Subject requests under Chapter III UK GDPR — including the right to be informed (Articles 13–14), access (Article 15), rectification (Article 16), erasure (Article 17), restriction (Article 18), data portability (Article 20), objection (Article 21), automated decision-making (Article 22), and withdrawal of consent (Article 7(3)). This implements Article 28(3)(e) and supports the Article 12 timing requirements.

If the Processor receives a request directly from a Data Subject, it shall acknowledge receipt, direct the Data Subject to the Controller, and promptly forward the request to the Controller. The Controller responds substantively.

The Controller acknowledges that certain rights are subject to the exemptions and derogations in **Schedules 2, 3, and 4 of the DPA 2018**, including those for crime and taxation, immigration, regulatory functions, journalism, research, and legal professional privilege. The Controller assesses whether an exemption applies. **OPTIONAL — only if the Services support law-enforcement or regulatory clients:** the Processor will provide configuration options to support DPA 2018 exemptions where technically feasible.

The Processor shall provide self-service tools to retrieve, correct, restrict, export, and delete Personal Data wherever practicable. Assistance beyond self-service that is manifestly disproportionate to routine operation may attract reasonable fees under the Principal Agreement.

Taking into account the nature of processing and the information available to the Processor, the Processor shall assist the Controller with its obligations under Articles 32–36 UK GDPR — security of processing (Article 32); breach notification to the ICO (Article 33); breach communication to Data Subjects (Article 34); DPIAs (Article 35); and prior consultation with the ICO (Article 36). ## 12. Personal data breach notification

This section sets out what the Processor will do if there is a security incident affecting Personal Data, so that the Controller can comply with its 72-hour notification duty.

The Processor shall notify the Controller without undue delay after becoming aware of a Personal Data Breach, and in any event within **{{Processor Breach Notification SLA}}** (default: **48 hours**). This implements Article 33(2) UK GDPR. The Controller acknowledges that under Article 33(1) it must notify the ICO of a notifiable Personal Data Breach without undue delay and, where feasible, not later than **72 hours** after becoming aware.

The Processor's notification shall include, to the extent then known: (a) the nature of the breach, including (where possible) the categories and approximate number of Data Subjects and records affected; (b) the name and contact details of the Processor's data-protection contact; (c) the likely consequences; and (d) the measures taken or proposed to address the breach and mitigate its effects. Information may be provided in phases in accordance with ICO guidance.

The Processor shall reasonably cooperate with the Controller's investigation, containment, and remediation, including by providing relevant logs, forensic information, and points of contact. The Controller is solely responsible for assessing notifiability under Articles 33–34 and for preparing and submitting any notifications. The Processor shall not publicly identify the Controller in connection with a breach without the Controller's prior written consent, except as required by law.

A Personal Data Breach does not include unsuccessful login attempts, port scans, denial-of-service activity not affecting availability of Personal Data, or other events that do not result in a confirmed compromise.

## 14. International data transfers

This section deals with what happens when Personal Data leaves the United Kingdom. It walks through the United Kingdom's full transfer toolkit: adequacy regulations, the IDTA, the UK Addendum to the EU SCCs, transfer risk assessment, binding corporate rules, and the Article 49 derogations.

### 14.1 Adequacy regulations

The Controller authorises the Processor to transfer Personal Data outside the United Kingdom to a country, territory, sector, or international organisation that is the subject of UK adequacy regulations made by the Secretary of State under section 17A of the DPA 2018. As of the Effective Date, the United Kingdom recognises adequacy in respect of: the EEA member states; Andorra; Argentina; Canada (commercial organisations subject to PIPEDA); the Faroe Islands; Guernsey; the Isle of Man; the State of Israel; Japan (private-sector organisations); Jersey; New Zealand; the Republic of Korea; Switzerland; and Uruguay. The United Kingdom also recognises a Data Bridge with the United States in respect of organisations certified to the UK Extension to the EU–US Data Privacy Framework (the "UK–US Data Bridge"). `[VERIFY — current adequacy list]`

The Controller acknowledges that the list of adequacy regulations is updated by the Secretary of State from time to time. The Processor shall update its transfer-mapping records (and Annex 3) as the list changes and shall implement an alternative transfer mechanism described in this clause where adequacy is withdrawn or qualified.

### 14.2 International Data Transfer Agreement (IDTA)

For Restricted Transfers that are not covered by adequacy regulations, the parties may rely on the Approved IDTA. The IDTA is the United Kingdom's standalone transfer mechanism. Its structure comprises four Tables — Tables 1 through 4 — together with Mandatory Clauses, optional Extra Protection Clauses, and Commercial Clauses. Where an IDTA is required for a Restricted Transfer under this DPA, the parties shall complete it in accordance with **Annex 4 §4.2**, and the IDTA so completed shall form part of this DPA. The Mandatory Clauses are incorporated by reference and shall not be amended except as permitted by their terms.

### 14.3 UK Addendum to EU SCCs

Where the Processor or the Controller already relies on the European Commission's Standard Contractual Clauses for transfers from the European Economic Area (Decision (EU) 2021/914), the parties may use the UK Addendum to extend those clauses to cover Restricted Transfers from the United Kingdom. The UK Addendum comprises four Tables — Table 1 (Parties), Table 2 (Selected SCCs, Modules, and Selected Clauses), Table 3 (Appendix Information), and Table 4 (Ending the Addendum if the Approved Addendum changes) — together with Mandatory Clauses incorporated by reference. Where the UK Addendum is required, the parties shall complete it in accordance with **Annex 4 §4.3**, and it shall form part of this DPA.

### 14.4 Transfer Risk Assessment (TRA)

Before relying on the IDTA, the UK Addendum, or any other transfer tool that requires it, the data exporter shall carry out a Transfer Risk Assessment ("TRA") to identify whether the transfer tool will, in the circumstances of the transfer, provide an essentially equivalent level of protection for the rights of Data Subjects in the destination country. The Processor shall provide reasonable information to assist the Controller's TRA, including information about access requests, the law and practice of relevant Restricted Countries to the extent the Processor has it, and any supplementary measures the Processor applies. The parties may use the ICO's Transfer Risk Assessment tool, or the alternative ICO-approved methodology, as the framework for the TRA. A skeleton TRA is included at **Annex 4 §4.4**.

### 14.5 Binding Corporate Rules

**OPTIONAL — only if the Processor or Sub-processor relies on Binding Corporate Rules:** the Processor may rely on Binding Corporate Rules approved by the ICO under Article 47 of the UK GDPR for intra-group Restricted Transfers. The current ICO authorisation reference, scope, and date are set out in **Annex 3** alongside the relevant Sub-processor entry.

### 14.6 Article 49 derogations

In the absence of adequacy regulations or appropriate safeguards, a Restricted Transfer may take place only on the basis of one of the derogations in Article 49 of the UK GDPR — for example, the Data Subject's explicit consent, transfer necessary for the performance of a contract with the Data Subject, or transfer necessary for important reasons of public interest. The parties acknowledge that Article 49 derogations are interpreted strictly by the ICO, are typically suitable only for occasional transfers, and shall not be used as a routine basis for Restricted Transfers.

## 15. Audits & inspections

This section explains how the Controller can verify the Processor's compliance with this DPA, balancing the Controller's right to audit with the practical realities of multi-tenant services.

The Processor shall make available to the Controller all information necessary to demonstrate compliance with the obligations laid down in Article 28 of the UK GDPR and shall allow for and contribute to audits, including inspections, conducted by the Controller or another auditor mandated by the Controller. This obligation implements Article 28(3)(h) of the UK GDPR.

Routine compliance shall be demonstrated by the Processor's provision, on reasonable request and subject to confidentiality undertakings, of: (a) its most recent SOC 2 Type II report; (b) its most recent ISO/IEC 27001:2022 certificate and Statement of Applicability; (c) where applicable, its NCSC Cyber Essentials or Cyber Essentials Plus certificate; (d) summary penetration test reports; (e) its current TOMs (Annex 2); (f) its current Sub-processor list (Annex 3); and (g) any other documentation reasonably necessary to evidence compliance with this DPA.

The Controller may, on at least **30 days'** prior written notice, conduct an on-site audit of the Processor's facilities and processing activities for cause — meaning following a Personal Data Breach affecting the Controller's Personal Data, an ICO inspection notice or enforcement action against the Controller in connection with the Services, or a documented and material concern about the Processor's compliance with this DPA that the parties have been unable to resolve through the routine compliance documentation. The audit shall be conducted: (i) during normal business hours; (ii) in a manner that minimises disruption to the Processor's operations; (iii) by appropriately qualified auditors who are not competitors of the Processor and who have signed appropriate confidentiality undertakings; and (iv) at the Controller's cost (save where the audit identifies a material breach of this DPA by the Processor).

The Processor shall cooperate with the ICO in the exercise of the ICO's lawful powers, including by providing access to premises, equipment, and Personal Data in accordance with section 146 and Schedule 15 of the DPA 2018, and shall promptly inform the Controller of any such regulatory contact relating to the Controller's Personal Data unless prohibited from doing so by law.

## 16. Return or deletion on termination

This section sets out what happens to Personal Data at the end of the engagement.

On termination or expiry of the Principal Agreement, or on the earlier completion of the relevant processing activity, the Processor shall — at the choice of the Controller exercised within **{{Return-or-Delete Election Window}}** (default: **30 days**) of termination — return all Personal Data to the Controller, or delete all Personal Data and existing copies, unless storage of the Personal Data is required by a law of the United Kingdom or any part of it to which the Processor is subject. This obligation implements Article 28(3)(g) of the UK GDPR.

Return shall be effected by export of Personal Data through the Services' standard export functionality or, where that is not reasonably practicable, by such other secure method as the parties agree in writing. Deletion shall be effected in accordance with the Processor's documented data-destruction procedures, aligned with NCSC guidance on secure sanitisation. Backup media and disaster-recovery copies shall be deleted in accordance with the Processor's standard rotation cycle, during which residual Personal Data shall remain subject to the TOMs and the confidentiality obligations in this DPA.

The Processor shall, on the Controller's reasonable written request, provide a written certificate of destruction signed by an authorised representative of the Processor confirming completion of deletion in accordance with this clause.

Where the Processor is required by law to retain Personal Data after termination, the Processor shall: (a) inform the Controller in writing of the relevant legal requirement and the categories of Personal Data retained, unless prohibited by law; (b) retain only the minimum Personal Data required; (c) continue to apply the TOMs to the retained Personal Data; (d) process the retained Personal Data only as required by the relevant law; and (e) delete the retained Personal Data once the legal retention requirement has expired. The ICO publishes guidance on retention which the Controller may consult to set its own retention schedules.

## 17. Liability & indemnity

This section coordinates the liability rules in the Principal Agreement with the statutory scheme of compensation and administrative fines under the UK GDPR.

The parties' liability under this DPA is governed by the limitation-of-liability provisions of the Principal Agreement, save that nothing in this DPA or the Principal Agreement excludes or limits liability that cannot lawfully be excluded or limited, including liability for: (a) death or personal injury caused by negligence; (b) fraud or fraudulent misrepresentation; (c) any other liability that cannot lawfully be excluded or limited under English law (or Scots law / Northern Irish law where selected under clause 18).

The parties acknowledge that, under Article 82 of the UK GDPR, any person who has suffered material or non-material damage as a result of an infringement of the UK GDPR has the right to receive compensation from the controller or processor for the damage suffered. Liability between the parties for any such compensation paid to a Data Subject shall be apportioned in accordance with each party's share of responsibility for the damage caused by the processing.

The parties further acknowledge that, under Article 83 of the UK GDPR and section 157 of the DPA 2018, the ICO may impose administrative fines for infringements. The standard maximum is the higher of **£8.7 million** or 2% of the undertaking's total worldwide annual turnover of the preceding financial year. The higher maximum is the higher of **£17.5 million** or 4% of the undertaking's total worldwide annual turnover of the preceding financial year. `[VERIFY — fine ceiling currency]` Each party is responsible for its own administrative fines, except to the extent that a fine arises directly from the other party's breach of this DPA.

**OPTIONAL — only if the Principal Agreement does not already address this:** each party shall indemnify the other against losses, damages, liabilities, and reasonable legal costs arising from third-party claims to the extent caused by the indemnifying party's breach of this DPA, subject to the indemnified party giving prompt written notice of the claim, granting the indemnifying party reasonable control of the defence and any settlement, and providing reasonable cooperation at the indemnifying party's expense.

## 19. Order of precedence & miscellaneous

This section explains how the various contractual documents fit together and houses the boilerplate that does not belong elsewhere.

**Order of precedence.** In the event of a conflict between the documents that govern the parties' relationship, the following order of precedence applies, save where Applicable Data Protection Law mandates otherwise: (a) the Mandatory Clauses of the IDTA or UK Addendum, where applicable, prevail over any conflicting term; (b) this DPA prevails over the Principal Agreement to the extent of any conflict relating to the processing of Personal Data; (c) the Annexes to this DPA prevail over the body of the DPA only to the extent the Annexes describe specific operational matters intended to govern the body's general statements; and (d) the Principal Agreement governs all other matters.

**Severability.** If any provision of this DPA is held to be invalid or unenforceable, the remaining provisions shall continue in full force and effect, and the parties shall negotiate in good faith to replace the invalid or unenforceable provision with a valid and enforceable provision that achieves to the greatest extent possible the original commercial intent.

**No third-party rights.** No person other than a party to this DPA shall have any rights under the Contracts (Rights of Third Parties) Act 1999 to enforce any term of this DPA, save that a Data Subject may enforce any rights expressly conferred on them by Applicable Data Protection Law and incorporated into this DPA by operation of law.

**Notices.** Notices given under this DPA shall be in writing and shall be sent to the addresses for notices designated in the Principal Agreement, with a copy to the relevant data-protection contact set out in the Principal Agreement or notified by the parties from time to time.

**Entire agreement.** This DPA, together with the Principal Agreement and the Annexes, constitutes the entire agreement between the parties relating to the processing of Personal Data and supersedes any previous data-processing agreement between the parties relating to the same subject matter.

**Amendments.** Amendments to this DPA shall be in writing and signed by an authorised representative of each party. The Processor may update the Annexes to reflect operational changes in accordance with this DPA without a counter-signed amendment, provided that the amendments do not materially reduce the level of protection afforded to Personal Data.

# Annex 2 — Technical and organisational measures (TOMs)

This Annex describes the security measures the Processor maintains, in accordance with Article 32 of the UK GDPR, the ICO's security guidance, NCSC Cyber Essentials, and ISO/IEC 27001:2022.

## A. Confidentiality

- **Access control.** Logical access to systems processing Personal Data is granted on a least-privilege, need-to-know basis. Access is provisioned and de-provisioned through a formal joiner-mover-leaver process. Privileged access is limited, monitored, and reviewed at least quarterly.
- **Authentication.** Multi-factor authentication is required for all administrative access to production systems, source-code repositories, and identity providers. Password complexity rules align with NCSC guidance.
- **Encryption in transit.** Personal Data is encrypted in transit using TLS 1.2 or higher with strong cipher suites. Internal service-to-service communication uses mutual TLS or equivalent.
- **Encryption at rest.** Personal Data is encrypted at rest using AES-256 or an algorithm of equivalent strength. Encryption keys are managed in a hardware security module or a managed key-management service with role-based access controls.
- **Pseudonymisation.** Where appropriate to the nature of the processing, Personal Data is pseudonymised, with the additional information needed to re-identify Data Subjects held separately and subject to its own access controls.

## B. Integrity

- **Change management.** Changes to production systems follow a documented change-management process including peer code review, automated testing, and approval workflows.
- **Vulnerability management.** Vulnerabilities are identified through static and dynamic application security testing, software composition analysis, and external penetration testing. Critical vulnerabilities are remediated within risk-based timelines aligned with NCSC guidance.
- **Logging and monitoring.** Security-relevant events are logged, time-synchronised, and retained for an appropriate period. Logs are protected against tampering and reviewed for indicators of compromise.

## C. Availability and resilience

- **Backups.** Personal Data is backed up on a regular schedule. Backups are encrypted and tested for restorability.
- **Disaster recovery.** Disaster-recovery plans address recovery time objectives and recovery point objectives appropriate to the Services. Plans are tested at least annually.
- **High availability.** Production systems are deployed across multiple availability zones or data-centres where supported by the Services.

## D. Procedures for regular testing, assessment, and evaluation

- **Independent assessment.** The Processor maintains certifications and reports including (as applicable) **ISO/IEC 27001:2022**, **SOC 2 Type II**, and **NCSC Cyber Essentials / Cyber Essentials Plus**, and shares summary versions on reasonable request under clause 15.
- **Internal audit.** The Processor's information-security function conducts internal audits on a risk-based schedule.
- **Risk management.** Risks to Personal Data are recorded in a risk register and reviewed at least annually.

## E. Personnel

- **Background screening.** Personnel with access to Personal Data are subject to background screening to the extent permitted by law.
- **Confidentiality.** Personnel are subject to written confidentiality obligations that survive termination.
- **Training.** Personnel receive data-protection and security awareness training at induction and at least annually thereafter.

## F. Incident response

- **Runbooks.** The Processor maintains documented incident-response runbooks covering detection, triage, containment, eradication, recovery, and post-incident review.
- **Tabletop exercises.** Tabletop exercises are conducted on a risk-based schedule.
- **Notification.** Personal Data Breaches are notified to the Controller in accordance with clause 12.

## G. Vendor and Sub-processor management

- **Due diligence.** Sub-processors are subject to due-diligence assessment before engagement and on a recurring basis thereafter.
- **Contractual flow-down.** Sub-processors are bound by written contracts imposing data-protection obligations no less protective than those in this DPA.
- **Continuous monitoring.** Sub-processor performance and security posture are monitored, including through review of certifications and incident communications.

## H. Audit

- **Cooperation.** The Processor cooperates with audits and inspections under clause 15.
- **Evidence.** The Processor maintains evidence of compliance sufficient to satisfy reasonable requests under clause 15.

## I. Special Category Data — additional safeguards

Where Personal Data includes Special Category Data, the Processor shall apply enhanced safeguards including stricter access controls, additional logging, and where appropriate, segregation from other data sets.

# Annex 4 — UK Transfer Mechanism Builder

This Annex collects the materials needed to put a transfer mechanism in place when Personal Data leaves the United Kingdom. It is structured so that the parties can (a) decide which mechanism applies, (b) complete the chosen instrument, and (c) carry out a Transfer Risk Assessment.

## 4.1 Selection guide — IDTA versus UK Addendum

The parties should answer the following questions to choose the right instrument.

**Step 1 — Is the destination covered by UK adequacy regulations?** If yes (see clause 14.1), no separate transfer instrument is required, although the Controller's transparency information and the parties' record of processing should reflect the transfer.

**Step 2 — If no, are EU SCCs already in place between the parties for the same data flow?** If yes, the most efficient path is to attach the **UK Addendum** to the existing EU SCCs (clause 14.3, Annex 4 §4.3). The UK Addendum extends the EU SCCs to cover Restricted Transfers from the United Kingdom by adding UK-specific Tables 1–4 and incorporating the Mandatory Clauses by reference.

**Step 3 — If EU SCCs are not in place — for example, the data flow is wholly outside the EEA's transfer regime — use the standalone Approved IDTA** (clause 14.2, Annex 4 §4.2). The IDTA is a single self-contained instrument covering Parties, Transfer Details, Transferred Data, Security Requirements, Mandatory Clauses, optional Extra Protection Clauses, and Commercial Clauses.

**Step 4 — For intra-group transfers within an authorised set of Binding Corporate Rules**, the BCRs themselves provide the transfer mechanism (clause 14.5), with the BCR authorisation reference recorded in Annex 3.

**Step 5 — For occasional transfers that meet a derogation in Article 49 of the UK GDPR**, no transfer instrument is required, but the parties must document the basis (clause 14.6). Article 49 should not be used as a routine basis.

In all cases (other than adequacy and Article 49 derogations relied on for occasional transfers), the data exporter must complete a Transfer Risk Assessment under §4.4.

## 4.2 IDTA — structure and skeleton

The Approved IDTA is divided into the following parts. Where the parties select the IDTA as the transfer mechanism, the parties shall complete a copy of the Approved IDTA published by the ICO. The Mandatory Clauses are incorporated by reference under the Open Government Licence. They are not reproduced here and shall not be amended save as their terms permit. `[VERIFY — current ICO Mandatory Clauses references]`

**Table 1 — Parties.** Identifies the data exporter and importer, key contact details for data protection, and the start date of the IDTA. The parties shall complete:
- exporter name, address, official registration (Companies House number, charity number, or equivalent), key contact, signature block;
- importer name, address, official registration (where applicable), key contact, signature block.

**Table 2 — Transfer Details.** Records the operative parameters of the transfer:
- the UK country whose laws apply (England & Wales, Scotland, or Northern Ireland) — see §4.5;
- the primary place of arbitration or court selected;
- the parties' status as controller or processor (controller-to-controller, controller-to-processor, processor-to-processor, processor-to-controller);
- whether the IDTA is linked to a "Linked Agreement" (typically the Principal Agreement and this DPA);
- the term of the IDTA (typically the term of the Principal Agreement plus any post-termination retention period required by clause 16);
- whether the importer may make further transfers and on what conditions;
- the review period for the Transfer Risk Assessment.

**Table 3 — Transferred Data.** Mirrors the Article 28 description in **Annex 1** and adds:

**Table 4 — Security Requirements.** Mirrors **Annex 2** and confirms:

**Extra Protection Clauses.** **OPTIONAL — only if the Transfer Risk Assessment identifies additional measures:** the parties may add Extra Protection Clauses to address risks identified in the TRA, for example, additional encryption, additional access-restriction commitments by the importer, or commitments to challenge unlawful access requests.

**Commercial Clauses.** **OPTIONAL — only if the Linked Agreement does not already address commercial terms:** the parties may add Commercial Clauses to address commercial matters not covered by the Principal Agreement, provided that those clauses do not conflict with the Mandatory Clauses.

## 4.4 Transfer Risk Assessment (TRA) — skeleton

The data exporter shall conduct a TRA before relying on the IDTA or the UK Addendum. The TRA may follow the ICO's Transfer Risk Assessment tool or the alternative ICO-approved methodology. The skeleton below captures the minimum elements.

1. **Description of the transfer.** Cross-reference Annex 1 and the relevant transfer instrument. Describe the data, the Data Subjects, the purposes, the importer, the importer's location, and the data flows to and from the importer.
2. **Importance of the transfer.** State why the transfer is necessary, the consequences of not transferring, and any alternatives considered.
3. **Identification of the destination's law and practice.** Identify the relevant local laws and practices that may affect the protection of the transferred data, including surveillance laws, government access powers, judicial remedies for non-residents, and the existence of an independent supervisory authority.
4. **Risk-based assessment.** Compare the local protections to the rights afforded by the UK GDPR, having regard to the categories of data, the sensitivity of the data, the volume of the data, and the likelihood of access by public authorities. Identify residual risks.
5. **Supplementary measures.** Identify any additional technical, organisational, or contractual measures that mitigate residual risks (for example, end-to-end encryption with keys held outside the destination, pseudonymisation, split processing, additional Extra Protection Clauses).
6. **Conclusion.** State whether the transfer instrument provides essentially equivalent protection in light of the supplementary measures, and document the rationale.
7. **Review trigger.** State the review period and the events that will trigger a fresh TRA (for example, change in the destination's surveillance law, change in the categories of data, change in supplementary measures, change in the importer).

# Optional Appendix — DUAA 2025 (Data (Use and Access) Act 2025)

`[VERIFY — DUAA 2025 phased commencement]`

This optional appendix flags the legislative changes introduced by the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 that may affect processor obligations. The DUAA 2025 received Royal Assent in 2025 and is being commenced in phases. Some provisions may not be in force at the Effective Date of this DPA and will only apply to the parties as and when they are brought into force by commencement regulations made by the Secretary of State.

The areas most relevant to processors include:

- **Records of processing (Article 30).** The DUAA 2025 modifies the threshold at which the obligation to maintain records of processing applies, with an emphasis on processing that is likely to result in a high risk to the rights and freedoms of Data Subjects. Where a Processor previously fell within the obligation only by virtue of the small-organisation exemption qualification, the Processor should review its record-keeping arrangements against the revised threshold once in force. `[VERIFY]`
- **Recognised legitimate interests.** The DUAA 2025 introduces a list of "recognised legitimate interests" — purposes for which controllers may rely on legitimate interests as a lawful basis without conducting the usual three-part balancing test. The list includes specific public-interest purposes set out in the DUAA 2025 itself. This change primarily affects controllers but may affect the Processor's documented instructions where the Controller's lawful basis changes. `[VERIFY]`
- **Solely automated decision-making (Article 22).** The DUAA 2025 reframes the rules on solely automated decision-making. Where the Services include solely automated decision-making, the parties should review the configuration and the Controller's transparency information against the revised regime. `[VERIFY]`
- **Data subject rights and complaints handling.** The DUAA 2025 introduces refinements to the response timetable and to the controller's complaint-handling obligations. The Processor's assistance under clause 10 will continue to be calibrated to the Controller's revised timeline. `[VERIFY]`
- **Cookie and PECR amendments.** The DUAA 2025 amends PECR in respect of low-risk cookies and analytics. To the extent the Services involve cookies or similar technologies, the Controller's notice and consent design should be reviewed against the revised regime. `[VERIFY]`
- **International transfers and the data-protection test.** The DUAA 2025 modifies the test the Secretary of State applies in making adequacy regulations and may affect the future scope of UK adequacy. The Processor will update Annex 3 as the adequacy list evolves. `[VERIFY]`

The parties shall review this DPA on a reasonable schedule following each commencement order under the DUAA 2025 and shall agree any necessary amendments in writing.
