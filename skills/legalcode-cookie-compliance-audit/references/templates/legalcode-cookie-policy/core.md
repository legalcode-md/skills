## 1. Plain-language summary

*Plain-language: A short, plain-English overview of how cookies work on this site and what choice you have.*

Cookies are small files that websites place on your device. Some are needed for the site to work at all — for example, to keep you logged in. Others are optional and help us measure how the service is used, remember your preferences, or show relevant content.

We use both kinds. The required ones run automatically because the service would break without them. The optional ones run only with your permission, where the law in your region requires us to ask first.

You control the optional ones. You can change your mind at any time through our cookie settings center at {{Cookie Settings URL}} or via your browser. Turning off optional cookies will not block you from using the core service, although some convenience features may behave differently.

The rest of this document covers what each category does, who the third-party providers are, how we record your choice, and how to contact us if something is unclear.

## 2. What cookies and similar technologies are

*Plain-language: "Cookies" is shorthand for several related technologies that store or read information on your device.*

When this policy says "cookies", it covers several related technologies. Most regulators treat them similarly, so we group them together.

- **Cookies** — small text files placed on your device and read back on later visits. Each is tied to a domain and has an expiry. A *session* cookie disappears when you close the browser; a *persistent* cookie survives until it expires or is deleted.
- **Local storage and session storage** — larger data slots inside your browser used to keep state between page loads. Functionally similar to cookies for privacy purposes.
- **IndexedDB** — a richer browser database used for offline functionality and structured caching.
- **Web beacons and pixels** — invisible images or scripts that report when a page or email is opened. Often paired with cookies for attribution.
- **Device identifiers and fingerprinting** — characteristics of your device (screen, fonts, timezone, language) that, combined, can identify a returning visitor without a stored cookie. Any fingerprinting use is disclosed in Annex A.
- **Mobile SDKs** — software libraries embedded in mobile apps that collect similar information to web cookies.

Where this policy says "set a cookie", read it as "set a cookie or use an equivalent technology above".

## 3. Why we use them

*Plain-language: We use cookies for a small number of clearly defined reasons.*

We use cookies and similar technologies to:

1. **Keep you signed in** and maintain session integrity across pages.
2. **Remember preferences** such as language, region, theme, and accessibility settings.
3. **Measure usage** so we can improve performance, fix bugs, and prioritise features.
4. **Protect the service** against fraud, abuse, automated attacks, and account takeover.
5. **Deliver and measure marketing** {{[OPTIONAL — only if advertising cookies present]}}, including relevant content on this site and on third-party platforms.

We do not use cookies to build hidden profiles for sale to data brokers, and we do not use them to make solely automated decisions that produce legal effects on you.

## 4. Categories of cookies we use

*Plain-language: We sort cookies into five categories. The first one runs automatically; the other four run only with your permission where required.*

### 4.1 Strictly necessary

Required for the service to function; cannot be switched off in our systems. Set in response to actions you take, such as logging in, submitting a form, or saving your privacy preferences.

- **Examples:** session identifiers, authentication tokens, CSRF tokens, load-balancer affinity, cookie-consent record.
- **Consent:** not required (strict-necessity exemption).

### 4.2 Functional / preferences

Remember choices you make so the service can be personalised.

- **Examples:** language and locale, theme, region, last-viewed content, accessibility settings.
- **Consent:** required where applicable law treats preference cookies as non-essential.

### 4.3 Analytics / performance

Help us understand how visitors interact with the service in aggregate.

- **Examples:** unique-visitor counts, page-view counts, navigation paths, error reports, performance timings.
- **Consent:** required.

### 4.4 Advertising / marketing

Set by us or by third-party advertising partners; build a record of your interests so advertising on this and other sites is more relevant.

- **Examples:** conversion pixels, retargeting tags, frequency-cap counters, click-attribution identifiers.
- **Consent:** required.
- **Conditional:** {{[OPTIONAL — only if advertising cookies present; otherwise state "We do not use advertising or marketing cookies."]}}

### 4.5 Social media

Set by social networks whose embeds, share buttons, or login widgets appear on the service.

- **Examples:** embedded-video cookies, share-button cookies, social-login cookies.
- **Consent:** required.
- **Conditional:** {{[OPTIONAL — only if social embeds present]}}

## 6. Third-party cookies & subprocessors

*Plain-language: Some cookies on this site are placed by other companies who provide services to us. Their categories are listed below.*

Third parties whose cookies may appear on the service fall into these groups:

- **Analytics providers** — measure usage on our behalf (acting as our processors).
- **Advertising providers** — show and measure adverts (typically separate or joint controllers).
- **Payment processors** — secure checkout and fraud prevention.
- **Customer support tools** — chat widgets and help-centre integrations.
- **Social media platforms** — embedded video, share buttons, social login.
- **Content delivery networks (CDNs)** — performance, caching, DDoS protection.

The specific providers, processing locations, and safeguards are in Annex A and in our subprocessor list at {{Subprocessor List URL}}. Our [Privacy Policy]({{Privacy Policy URL}}) describes the broader arrangement with each provider.

**Legal status note.** Third-party cookies do not all share data on the same legal footing. Where a provider acts on our written instructions for purposes we set (typically analytics and support), it is our **processor** and the relationship is governed by a data-processing agreement. Where a provider sets cookies for its own purposes (typically advertising and social media), it is a **separate controller**, and you should also consult that provider's own notice. Annex A flags which status applies.

## 7. Your choices & how to manage cookies

*Plain-language: You have three ways to control cookies on this site.*

You have the following mechanisms to control cookies:

**(a) Cookie settings center.** The simplest route is {{Cookie Settings URL}}. It lets you grant or withdraw consent by category. Choices take effect immediately and are remembered for at least six months.

**(b) Browser controls.** Every modern browser lets you block, delete, or be warned about cookies. See §8. Browser controls affect this site and every other site you visit.

**(c) Third-party advertising opt-outs.** {{[OPTIONAL — only if advertising cookies present]}} For interest-based advertising delivered through industry programmes:
- **Network Advertising Initiative:** {{NAI Opt-Out URL}}
- **Digital Advertising Alliance:** {{DAA Opt-Out URL}}
- **EDAA — Your Online Choices:** {{EDAA Opt-Out URL}}

Industry opt-outs cover participating networks only; they stop interest-based targeting by members but do not stop all advertising.

Withdrawing consent is as easy as giving it. Withdrawal does not affect processing that happened beforehand.

## 9. Global Privacy Control (GPC) & Do Not Track (DNT)

*Plain-language: GPC is a modern opt-out signal we honour where the law requires; DNT is an older signal we treat as a non-binding preference.*

**Global Privacy Control (GPC).** GPC is a signal a browser or extension sends on every request to indicate you do not want personal information sold or shared, and that you want to opt out of targeted advertising. Where applicable law treats GPC as a valid opt-out (for example, several US state privacy laws), we honour it automatically and treat it as a withdrawal of consent for the advertising and certain analytics categories in §4.

**Do Not Track (DNT).** DNT is an older browser signal that was never adopted as a binding standard. Most regulators do not require sites to honour it. We treat DNT as {{DNT Honor Stance}} — by default, not as a separate legal opt-out, but as a non-binding preference signal.

Where a jurisdictional overlay to this policy requires us to honour DNT or any other signal, that overlay controls.

## 10. How we obtain and record consent

*Plain-language: In regions that require consent, we ask before setting non-essential cookies and we keep a record of your choice.*

In regions requiring opt-in consent for non-essential cookies:

- **Before** any non-essential cookie is set, we present a banner that lets you accept all, reject all, or manage by category.
- The banner pre-ticks no non-essential category, and "reject" is as prominent as "accept".
- For each consent event we record: a pseudonymous user identifier, timestamp, banner version, banner language, categories accepted, and categories rejected.
- Consent records are retained for at least 12 months and are accessible to the user on request.
- We re-prompt when a category materially changes (e.g., a new advertising vendor) or after 12 months, whichever comes first.

In regions operating on an opt-out or notice-and-choice basis, we present a clear notice on first visit and provide the opt-out mechanisms in §7. We honour valid opt-out signals, including GPC where required.

The legal bases for non-consent processing of strictly necessary cookies (legitimate interests / contract performance / legal obligation, depending on jurisdiction) are in our [Privacy Policy]({{Privacy Policy URL}}).

## Annex B — Consent banner UX requirements (jurisdiction-neutral baseline)

*Plain-language: For the team building the consent banner. It sets the floor; per-jurisdiction overlays raise it.*

This is the **baseline** any consent banner deployed on the service must meet. Per-jurisdiction overlays (EEA/UK, California, Brazil, etc.) may impose stricter requirements; where they conflict, the stricter rule wins.

**B.1 First-visit behaviour**
- Banner appears on first visit, before any non-essential cookie or similar technology is set.
- No non-essential cookie reads or writes occur until the user has chosen.

**B.2 Equal prominence**
- "Accept all" and "Reject all" have equal visual prominence: same contrast, size, affordance, and position weight.
- "Manage preferences" is at least as prominent as "Accept all".
- No dark patterns: no nudge colours, no buried reject, no friction asymmetry.

**B.3 Granular control**
- Consent can be granted or withheld **per category** as defined in §4.
- Each category has a one-line, plain-English purpose description.
- No pre-ticked checkboxes for any non-essential category.

**B.4 Persistence**
- The user's choice is stored for **at least 6 months** (stricter cap may apply per overlay).
- Choice is honoured across pages, in-scope subdomains, and sessions until it expires or changes.

**B.5 Withdrawal**
- A persistent link to the cookie settings center sits in the site footer at all times.
- Withdrawing consent is no harder than granting it (same clicks or fewer).

**B.6 Record of consent**
- Each event logs: pseudonymous user identifier, UTC timestamp, banner version, language, categories accepted, categories rejected, and mechanism (banner / settings center / API).
- Logs are retained for at least 12 months and are tamper-evident.

**B.7 Re-prompting**
- Re-prompt when a category materially changes (new vendor, purpose, or legal basis).
- Re-prompt at least every 12 months.
- Do not re-prompt purely to wear the user into accepting (that is itself a dark pattern).

**B.8 Accessibility**
- Banner meets WCAG 2.2 AA: keyboard-operable, screen-reader-labelled, sufficient contrast, no time-out on the choice.
- Plain-language copy at no higher than {{Reading Level Target}} (default: B1 / 8th grade).

**B.9 Signal handling**
- Honour GPC where applicable law requires.
- Treat DNT per §9.
- For returning users with a prior opt-out signal, respect it on first load (no fresh "accept all" prompt that overrides it).
