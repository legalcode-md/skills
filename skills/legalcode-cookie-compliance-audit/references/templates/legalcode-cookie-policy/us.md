## 2. What cookies and similar technologies are

This section explains the technologies in plain English.

A **cookie** is a small text record that a website asks your browser to keep. On a later visit the saved record is sent back, letting the recipient recognize the browser or retrieve preferences. Where this policy refers to "cookies," it includes:

- **Local and session storage** maintained by your browser;
- **Pixel tags, web beacons, and tracking pixels** — tiny image or script elements that report a load back to a server;
- **SDKs** embedded in any companion mobile or desktop application;
- **Server-side identifiers** linked to a browser via a first-party cookie;
- **Device or browser fingerprinting signals** where used (disclosed in Annex A).

Cookies are classified by **duration** (session vs. persistent) and by **origin** (first-party set by {{Website URL}}; third-party set by another loaded domain).

## 3. Why we use them

This section sets out the purposes cookies serve and flags which trigger CCPA opt-out rights.

- **Operate the site.** Strictly necessary cookies authenticate sessions, balance load, prevent fraud, and remember in-flight cart or form data.
- **Remember preferences.** Functional cookies store language, region, accessibility, and dismissed-notice state.
- **Measure performance.** Analytics cookies aggregate page visits, errors, and feature usage.
- **Improve and secure features.** Cookies support A/B testing, feature flags, abuse and bot detection, and rate limiting.
- **Provide advertising.** Where enabled and not opted out, advertising and re-targeting cookies reach you on third-party sites and measure conversions.

**CCPA Sale and Sharing flag.** Some uses — typically cookies set or read by advertising and certain measurement partners — may constitute a "Sale" under §1798.140(ad) or "Sharing" under §1798.140(ah) (cross-context behavioral advertising). Sharing captures disclosure for targeted advertising even where no money changes hands. Annex A flags each cookie's status under our current configuration.

Analytics cookies deployed through a provider acting as a **service provider** under §1798.140(ag) — bound by a written contract restricting retention, use, and onward disclosure to the services we have requested — are generally not treated as Sale or Sharing.

## 4. Categories of cookies we use

This section maps our five cookie categories to CCPA terminology.

**4.1 Strictly necessary.** Required to deliver a requested service — login, checkout, session security, accessibility. Not subject to opt-in or opt-out. CCPA: **outside** Sale or Sharing.

**4.2 Functional / preference.** Remember non-essential choices like language or layout. CCPA: typically **outside** Sale or Sharing where the provider is a service provider.

**4.3 Analytics / performance.** Measure how visitors interact with the site. Where the provider is bound as a **service provider** under §1798.140(ag), processing is on our behalf and is **not** Sale or Sharing. Where a measurement partner uses the data for its own purposes, the cookie falls under Sharing. Annex A records which model applies per provider.

**4.4 Targeted advertising / cross-context behavioral advertising.** Build or read profiles to deliver advertising on other sites and apps based on activity across unaffiliated services. Within **Sharing** under §1798.140(ah); subject to opt-out. Off by default for users with a valid opt-out (including GPC) and for users we know to be under 16.

**4.5 Social media and embedded content.** Loaded when you interact with embedded media (video, feed, share button). May set a third-party cookie permitting cross-site recognition. May constitute Sharing depending on the partner's role.

## 6. Third-party cookies & subprocessors

This section explains who else can read cookies on our pages and how their CCPA relationship affects your rights.

Third parties on {{Website URL}} fall under one of three CCPA relationship types. The classification controls whether the disclosure is Sale or Sharing.

**6.1 Service providers (§1798.140(ag)).** Vendors processing personal information on our behalf under a written contract that restricts retention, use, and onward disclosure to the services we have requested, bars selling or sharing, and bars combining the data with other sources except as regulation permits. Cookies set by service providers — hosting, error monitoring, consent platform, most analytics — are **not** Sale or Sharing.

**6.2 Contractors (§1798.140(j)).** A parallel category — a person to whom we make personal information available for a business purpose under an equivalent contract. We treat contractors with the same operational controls as service providers.

**6.3 Third parties (§1798.140(ai)).** The residual category. Disclosure to a third party is Sale or Sharing and triggers opt-out rights. Most advertising and re-targeting partners fall here unless contractually downgraded.

Annex A records, for each cookie: vendor, relationship type, Sale/Sharing status, and a link to the vendor's privacy notice.

## 8. Browser controls

This section covers browser-level controls independent of our preference center.

Every modern browser blocks, prompts, or deletes cookies. Controls operate per-browser and per-device and do not propagate unless you sign into the browser. Blocking strictly necessary cookies will break parts of {{Website URL}}.

- **Google Chrome.** Settings → Privacy and security → Third-party cookies. For GPC, install an extension listed at globalprivacycontrol.org.
- **Mozilla Firefox.** Settings → Privacy & Security → Enhanced Tracking Protection. Enable **"Tell websites not to sell or share my data"** to send a GPC signal.
- **Apple Safari.** Settings → Privacy → "Prevent cross-site tracking." Safari 17+ and iOS Safari include a GPC signal under Advanced Tracking and Fingerprinting Protection in private browsing.
- **Microsoft Edge.** Settings → Cookies and site permissions. GPC available via Chromium extensions.
- **Brave.** Settings → Shields → "Tell sites not to sell or share my data" sends GPC by default.

To clear cookies for {{Website URL}} alone, click the lock icon in the address bar and select "Cookies and site data."

## 9. Global Privacy Control (GPC) & Do Not Track (DNT)

This section explains how we treat GPC and why we do not treat DNT as binding.

**9.1 Global Privacy Control (GPC).** GPC, defined at globalprivacycontrol.org, transmits a "Sec-GPC: 1" header and a JavaScript property indicating no consent to Sale or Sharing. From a visitor in a covered state we treat GPC as a valid opt-out from Sale or Sharing **and** from targeted advertising. We apply it to the browser session immediately; for signed-in users we link it to the account so the opt-out follows the user across devices.

GPC is recognized as a universal opt-out mechanism in these states:

- **California** — Cal. Civ. Code §1798.135(b) and the CPPA Final Regulations.
- **Colorado** — Colo. Rev. Stat. §6-1-1306 and the Colorado AG UOOM List; effective **2024-07-01**.
- **Connecticut** — Conn. Gen. Stat. §42-518; effective **2025-01-01**.
- **Texas** — Tex. Bus. & Com. Code §541.055(c); effective **2025-01-01** `[VERIFY]`.
- **Oregon** — OCPA; effective **2026-01-01** `[VERIFY]`.

For other US users, we honor GPC as a matter of policy where technically feasible.

**9.2 Do Not Track (DNT).** DNT is an older browser header. The FTC and most state regulators have not adopted it as a binding opt-out under modern statutes, and the W3C never finalized it. We do **not** treat DNT as binding. We may treat it as a non-binding preference. Under CalOPPA we disclose this approach as required.

**9.3 Conflicts.** Where a GPC signal conflicts with a later in-product choice, we surface the conflict and ask the user to confirm the more recent choice, consistent with the CPPA Final Regulations.

## 10. How we obtain and record consent

This section describes the US consent model and our record-keeping.

The default US model is **opt-out**. Sale/Sharing cookies default to **on** for adult users until opt-out. Strictly necessary cookies need no consent. Two situations require opt-in:

- **Sensitive personal information.** Where a cookie collects sensitive PI beyond what §1798.121 allows without a limit-use right, we obtain affirmative opt-in.
- **Children.** Under 16, opt-in is required before Sale or Sharing. For 13–15, we collect the user's own opt-in under §1798.120(c). For under 13, we collect verifiable parental consent meeting COPPA at 15 U.S.C. §§6501–6506 and 16 C.F.R. Part 312, including the methods at §312.5.

**Record of consent.** For every banner interaction we record: visitor and account identifier, timestamp and time zone, categories accepted/declined/default, GPC signal received and value, banner and policy version, and IP truncated to network prefix with derived country.

We retain these records for at least two years to demonstrate compliance with §1798.130 and the CPPA record-keeping rules, and surface them on request.

## 15. State-specific addenda

The addenda below restate the rights specific to residents of named states. The substantive rights described above apply across the named states; this section captures unique mechanics or thresholds.

### 15.1 California

California residents have the full set of CCPA rights at §§1798.100–1798.199.100 — Right to Know, Right to Delete, Right to Correct, Right to Limit Use of Sensitive Personal Information, and Right to Opt Out of Sale or Sharing. Use the channels in §7. California has additionally maintained the older **"Shine the Light" law** at Cal. Civ. Code §1798.83, which gives California residents the right to request, once per year and at no charge, a list of the categories of personal information we have shared with third parties for those third parties' direct marketing purposes during the immediately preceding calendar year, together with the names and addresses of those third parties. Submit a Shine the Light request to **{{Company Email Privacy}}** with the subject "Shine the Light Request."

### 15.2 Colorado

Colorado residents have CPA opt-out rights under Colo. Rev. Stat. §§6-1-1301 et seq. for targeted advertising, sale, and certain profiling. Colorado recognizes a **Universal Opt-Out Mechanism (UOOM)** — including GPC — that we honor effective **2024-07-01** under the Colorado Attorney General's UOOM Public List. Submit other CPA requests through **{{Privacy Request Portal URL}}** or by emailing **{{Company Email Privacy}}**; the Colorado AG's office handles enforcement appeals.

### 15.3 Connecticut

Connecticut residents have CTDPA rights under Conn. Gen. Stat. §§42-515 to 42-525, including opt-out of targeted advertising, sale, and profiling with significant effects. Connecticut requires honoring an **opt-out preference signal** — including GPC — effective **2025-01-01** per §42-518. Submit other CTDPA requests through **{{Privacy Request Portal URL}}**. Enforcement is exclusively by the Connecticut Attorney General.

### 15.4 Texas

Texas residents have TDPSA rights under Tex. Bus. & Com. Code §§541.001 et seq., including opt-out of targeted advertising, sale, and profiling. Texas recognizes a **universal opt-out mechanism** under §541.055(c), effective **2025-01-01** `[VERIFY]`. We honor GPC for Texas residents on that basis. The Texas Attorney General's Consumer Protection Division enforces the TDPSA. `[VERIFY]` whether and how the Texas AG has issued guidance on GPC specifically before relying on this clause.

### 15.5 Oregon

Oregon residents have OCPA rights under ORS §§646A.570 et seq., including opt-out of targeted advertising, sale, and profiling with significant effects. Oregon requires honoring **opt-out preference signals** — including GPC — effective **2026-01-01** for controllers that meet OCPA's thresholds. We honor GPC for Oregon residents on that basis. Enforcement is exclusively by the Oregon Attorney General.

## Annex B — Consent banner UX requirements (US opt-out / GPC honoring)

This annex captures the operational requirements that satisfy the CCPA, the CPPA Final Regulations, and parallel state UOOM rules.

**B.1 Notice at Collection.** A plain-language Notice at Collection must appear at or before the cookie-set event — typically the banner on first visit — listing categories of PI collected, purposes, retention, and links to this Cookie Policy and the main Privacy Policy.

**B.2 Equal prominence.** "Accept" and "Reject" / "Do Not Sell or Share" choices must have equal prominence — same weight, size, position — per the CPPA Final Regulations.

**B.3 No dark patterns.** No layout or flow that substantially subverts user autonomy. Avoid: pre-selected non-essential toggles, buried reject buttons, forced flows without a "Reject all" path, double negatives.

**B.4 Persistent footer links.** "Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information" (or equivalent state phrasing) → **{{Do Not Sell URL}}** and "Cookie Settings" → **{{Cookie Settings URL}}** must appear on every page.

**B.5 GPC handling.** When a request carries GPC: (1) treat the browser as opted out of Sale/Sharing immediately; (2) link the opt-out to a signed-in account; (3) reflect the state in the banner, do not re-prompt for advertising consent; (4) log header, JS property, and timestamp.

**B.6 Under-16 flow.** When age is known or inferred under 16, default off and require opt-in. Under 13: block until verifiable parental consent through {{Parental Consent Verification URL}} per 16 C.F.R. §312.5. 13–15: present an opt-in dialog under §1798.120(c).

**B.7 Authorized agent path.** Surface a link to **{{Authorized Agent Submission URL}}** in the banner and preference center.

**B.8 Record-keeping.** Retain, for each interaction: visitor identifier, categories chosen, GPC signal value, IP prefix and derived state, banner and policy version, UTC timestamp. Retain at least 24 months.

**B.9 Re-engagement.** Do not re-prompt an opted-out user within 12 months absent a material change. The Cookie Settings link remains available.

**B.10 Accessibility.** Conform to WCAG 2.1 AA — keyboard-operable, screen-reader-labeled, focus-visible.
