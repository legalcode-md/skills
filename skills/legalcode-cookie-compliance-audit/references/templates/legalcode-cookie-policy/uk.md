## 1. Plain-language summary

*Plain-language: A short overview of how cookies work on this service in the UK and what choices you have under PECR and the UK GDPR.*

In the UK, cookies are governed by **PECR Regulation 6**, which forbids storing or reading information on a user's terminal equipment without prior consent — unless that storage or access is strictly necessary for a service the user has asked for. PECR borrows its definition of consent from the **UK GDPR**, so the consent we ask for must meet **Articles 6(1)(a) and 7**: freely given, specific, informed, unambiguous, and as easy to withdraw as to give.

In practice, that means three things. The cookies that keep you signed in, secure your session, and record your consent run automatically because the law treats them as strictly necessary. Every other category — preferences, analytics, advertising, and social media — is off until you switch it on. You can change your mind at any time through our cookie settings centre at {{Cookie Settings URL}}, with no more friction than acceptance.

We follow the **Information Commissioner's Office** ("ICO") cookies guidance as updated in 2023, including equal prominence for "Accept all" and "Reject all" in the first layer. We track the ICO's evolving position on first-party analytics and the phased commencement of DUAA 2025 `[VERIFY current ICO position post-DUAA 2025]`.

## 2. What cookies and similar technologies are

*Plain-language: PECR covers more than text-file cookies; it covers any technology that stores or reads information on your device.*

PECR Regulation 6 applies whenever a person stores information on, or gains access to information stored in, a user's terminal equipment. The drafting is technology-neutral. The **ICO 2023 cookies guidance** confirms the same rules apply to:

- **First- and third-party cookies** — small text files placed and read by a domain.
- **Local storage, session storage, IndexedDB** — browser data slots for state and caching.
- **Pixel tags, web beacons, tracking GIFs** — invisible images or scripts that report opens and views.
- **SDK identifiers** in mobile apps and connected devices.
- **Device fingerprinting** — combining device characteristics to identify a returning user without a stored cookie. The ICO is consistent that fingerprinting is in scope.
- **Link decoration and similar bridging techniques** that cause information to be stored on or read from the device.

Where this overlay says "set a cookie", read it as "use any of the technologies above". The legal test is what is stored or read, not what it is called.

## 3. Why we use them

*Plain-language: Five short reasons, framed by what PECR Reg. 6 lets us do without consent.*

We use cookies and similar technologies to:

1. **Keep you signed in** — strictly necessary under PECR Reg. 6(4).
2. **Protect the service** against fraud, abuse, and account takeover — strictly necessary where the sole purpose is to preserve the requested service.
3. **Remember your preferences** (language, region, theme, accessibility) — consent required.
4. **Measure how the service is used** so we can improve it — consent required.
5. **Deliver and measure marketing** {{[OPTIONAL — only if advertising cookies present]}} — consent required, with the ICO direct-marketing guidance applied.

We do not build hidden behavioural profiles for sale to data brokers, do not use cookie-derived signals for solely automated decisions with legal effects, and do not deploy fingerprinting to circumvent your stated cookie choice.

## 4. Categories of cookies we use

*Plain-language: Five categories, with PECR Regulation 6 treatment for each.*

### 4.1 Strictly necessary

Required for the service to function. Exempt under **PECR Reg. 6(4)** — "strictly necessary for the provision of an information society service requested by the subscriber or user".

- **Examples:** session identifiers, authentication tokens, CSRF tokens, load-balancer affinity, the consent record itself.
- **Consent:** not required.
- **ICO note:** the test is read narrowly. Convenience or business-improvement is not enough.

### 4.2 Functional / preferences

Remember choices you make.

- **Examples:** language and locale, theme, region, last-viewed content, accessibility settings.
- **Consent:** required. The ICO does not treat preference cookies as strictly necessary.

### 4.3 Analytics / performance

Help us understand how visitors interact with the service in aggregate.

- **Examples:** unique visitor counts, page-view counts, navigation paths, error reports, performance timings.
- **Consent:** required. The ICO has indicated openness to greater flexibility for first-party audience-measurement analytics where data are not shared with third parties and are used solely to improve the service. We continue to ask for consent while that position settles `[VERIFY current ICO position post-DUAA 2025]`.

### 4.4 Advertising / marketing

Set by us or by advertising partners to build a record of interests for relevant advertising.

- **Examples:** conversion pixels, retargeting tags, frequency-cap counters, click-attribution identifiers.
- **Consent:** required. Cross-reference the **ICO direct-marketing guidance** (PECR Regs. 22–23 floor for electronic marketing).
- **Conditional:** {{[OPTIONAL — only if advertising cookies present; otherwise state "We do not use advertising or marketing cookies."]}}

### 4.5 Social media

Set by social networks via embeds, share buttons, or login widgets.

- **Examples:** embedded video player cookies, share-button cookies, social-login cookies.
- **Consent:** required.
- **Joint-controllership flag:** where a social platform sets cookies for its own purposes via an embed on our pages, we and the platform may be **joint controllers** under **UK GDPR Article 26**. See the companion `legalcode-dpa`.
- **Conditional:** {{[OPTIONAL — only if social embeds present]}}

## 9. Global Privacy Control (GPC) & Do Not Track (DNT)

*Plain-language: Neither GPC nor DNT is currently a binding signal in the UK, but we may treat GPC as a non-binding preference.*

**Global Privacy Control (GPC).** GPC signals that a user does not want personal data sold or shared and does not want behavioural advertising. As at the date of this overlay, GPC is **not a binding opt-out signal under PECR or the UK GDPR**, and the ICO has not issued formal guidance treating it as such `[VERIFY current ICO position post-DUAA 2025]`. We treat GPC as a non-binding preference: on receiving it, we may decline to set non-essential cookies and may pre-populate the banner with non-essential categories rejected. Override is available in the cookie settings centre.

**Do Not Track (DNT).** Never adopted as a binding standard. The ICO does not require it to be honoured. We treat DNT as informational only.

**DUAA 2025 and future signals.** DUAA 2025 introduces a framework for recognised opt-out signals and may, on commencement, give specific browser signals statutory weight `[VERIFY DUAA 2025 commencement]`. We will update this section when the position settles.

## 10. How we obtain and record consent

*Plain-language: Banner first. Equal prominence. Granular. Recorded.*

We obtain and record consent in line with **PECR Regulation 6**, **UK GDPR Articles 6(1)(a) and 7**, and the **ICO 2023 cookies guidance**:

- **Banner before first use.** Shown on first visit, before any non-essential cookie is set.
- **Equal prominence.** "Accept all" and "Reject all" in the first layer with identical size, colour-contrast, affordance, layout, and position weight. "Manage preferences" with at least equal prominence to "Accept all".
- **No pre-ticked boxes.** No non-essential category pre-ticked or default-on (*Planet49*; ICO guidance).
- **Granular per category.** Consent requested at the category level in §4; per-vendor control where a category mixes vendors with materially different purposes.
- **No cookie walls.** Access is not conditioned on accepting non-essential cookies, save in narrow circumstances meeting the ICO's compelling-reasons standard with an equivalent service or opt-out path. {{[OPTIONAL — delete unless a cookie wall is in use.]}}
- **Record of consent.** Each event logged with pseudonymous user ID, UTC timestamp, banner version, categories accepted, categories rejected, banner language, and consent mechanism. Retained **at least 12 months** and accessible to the user on request.
- **Re-prompt cadence.** On material change or every **12 months**, whichever comes first.
- **Withdrawal.** Persistent footer link on every page; no harder than acceptance.

**ICO enforcement.** PECR contraventions can be enforced through information, assessment, and enforcement notices, and monetary penalties. Where the same processing also breaches the UK GDPR, the ICO may impose monetary penalties up to the **UK GDPR fine ceiling — £17.5 million or 4% of total annual worldwide turnover, whichever is higher** `[VERIFY ceiling post-DUAA 2025]`. The ICO accepts undertakings from organisations that come into compliance, and in 2023 wrote to a number of the most-visited UK websites about non-compliant banners `[VERIFY recent ICO actions]`.

## 12. Children's data

*Plain-language: No targeted ads or behavioural analytics for users we know to be children. The Children's Code applies if the service is likely to be accessed by children.*

**UK GDPR Article 8 and DPA 2018 s.9** set the age below which a child cannot give their own consent for an information-society service at **13**. Where consent under §10 is the only lawful path for a non-essential cookie set on a user we know to be under 13, we suppress it unless given or authorised by a person with parental responsibility.

If the service is **likely to be accessed by children** within the meaning of the **ICO Age Appropriate Design Code** ("Children's Code"), its 15 standards apply. In particular:

- **Standard 7 — Default settings:** high-privacy by default.
- **Standard 11 — Profiling:** off by default; no behavioural advertising cookies for users we know to be under 18 where identifiable; retargeting and frequency-cap cookies suppressed for those users.
- **Standard 12 — Nudge techniques:** no nudging children towards lower-privacy choices.

See §13 of our [Privacy Policy]({{Privacy Policy URL}}) for the full account.

## 15. DUAA 2025 — Forward-looking flag

*Plain-language: DUAA 2025 changes parts of the UK regime; some provisions are in force, some are not.* `[VERIFY — DUAA 2025 phased commencement; some provisions may not be in force at adoption.]`

The **Data (Use and Access) Act 2025** ("DUAA 2025") amends parts of the UK GDPR, DPA 2018, and PECR, and is being commenced in phases. Until each provision is in force, the prior law applies. The provisions most relevant here:

- **New PECR exceptions for low-risk analytics.** DUAA 2025 introduces or extends exceptions to PECR Reg. 6 for cookies whose sole purpose is statistical information about service use, where data are not shared with third parties or used to identify individuals. Where in force and applicable, we may rely on it instead of consent and mark Annex A accordingly `[VERIFY scope]`.
- **Recognised legitimate interests** under UK GDPR Article 6(1)(f). None of these displaces the PECR Reg. 6 consent requirement for cookies — PECR is a separate regime `[VERIFY final list]`.
- **Opt-out signal recognition.** The Secretary of State may designate binding browser-based opt-out signals; we will update §9 when one is named `[VERIFY designations]`.
- **ICO enforcement.** DUAA 2025 expands ICO investigation tools and revises penalty mechanics; the §10 ceiling may change `[VERIFY post-commencement ceiling]`.
- **International transfers.** Adequacy and transfer-safeguard frameworks are amended. See the companion `legalcode-dpa`.

## Annex B — Consent banner UX requirements (ICO 2023-aligned)

*Plain-language: This annex tells the team building the banner exactly what the ICO 2023 guidance requires.*

The following is the **UK floor** for any consent banner deployed on the service. Where the jurisdiction-neutral baseline is stricter, the stricter rule applies.

**B.1 First visit.** Banner shown before any non-essential cookie is set. No non-essential reads or writes until the user makes a choice or sends a recognised opt-out signal.

**B.2 Equal prominence (ICO 2023).** "Accept all" and "Reject all" in the first layer with identical size, contrast, affordance, layout, and position weight. "Manage preferences" with at least equal prominence to "Accept all". No nudge colours, buried reject controls, friction asymmetry, auto-scroll past the choice, or time pressure.

**B.3 No pre-ticked boxes.** No non-essential category pre-ticked or default-on (*Planet49*; ICO guidance).

**B.4 Granular.** Per-category control as in §4 with one-line plain-English purposes; per-vendor control behind "Manage preferences" where a category mixes vendors with materially different purposes.

**B.5 Persistent access.** Footer link to the cookie settings centre on every page.

**B.6 Record of consent.** Each event: pseudonymous user ID, UTC timestamp, banner version, language, categories accepted/rejected, and mechanism. Retained **at least 12 months**, tamper-evident.

**B.7 Re-prompt.** On material change or at least every **12 months**. No wear-down re-prompts.

**B.8 Cookie wall.** Not used by default. {{[OPTIONAL — delete unless in use.]}} Where used, must meet the ICO's compelling-reasons standard with an equivalent service or opt-out path.

**B.9 Accessibility.** WCAG 2.2 AA: keyboard-operable, screen-reader-labelled, sufficient contrast, no time-out. Reading level no higher than {{Reading Level Target}} (default: B1 / Year 9).

**B.10 Signal handling.** GPC treated as a non-binding preference per §9 pending a binding designation under DUAA 2025 `[VERIFY]`; DNT informational only. A returning user's prior rejection is respected on first load.

**B.11 ICO supervision.** The ICO has signalled enforcement intent on non-compliant banners and corresponded with several top UK websites since 2023 `[VERIFY recent ICO actions]`. Banner reviews repeat at least every 12 months and after each material change.
